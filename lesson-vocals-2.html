Vocal Recording Techniques: Capturing the Performance

Beyond equipment, recording technique determines whether you capture a great performance or a technical disaster. This lesson covers microphone technique, multiple takes for comping, headphone mixes, and the art of directing a vocalist. The goal: capture raw material that's easy to edit and mix.

Multiple Takes

Record 3-5 takes minimum. No vocalist nails every word on one take. Multiple takes give you options for comping—choosing the best moments from each take to create one perfect performance.

Headphone Mix

Give the singer what they need to hear. Usually: track minus lead vocal, plus reverb on their voice for comfort. If they can't hear themselves well, they'll sing off-pitch or strain.

Consistent Distance

Maintain mic distance. Moving closer/further changes tone and volume dramatically. Mark a spot with tape. Coach the vocalist to stay consistent—especially during loud parts.

Punch-In Recording

Fix specific sections. Don't re-record entire takes for one mistake. Punch in on the problem line, letting the vocalist hear context before and after. This saves time and preserves energy.

Session Tips

  • Warm up: Let the vocalist do scales or easy songs first
  • Take breaks: Vocal fatigue is real—rest every 30-45 minutes
  • Stay positive: Singers perform better when they feel confident
  • Label takes: Note which takes have the best moments
