Sub Bass Layering: Weight + Clarity

Layering sub bass with mid bass is the professional approach to bass production. Pure sub-bass (a sine wave) provides weight you feel in your chest but is nearly inaudible on small speakers. Mid-bass provides the note definition and character but lacks physical impact. Layer them together, and you get the best of both: power on big systems and clarity on everything else.

The Sub Layer

Pure sine wave, no harmonics. The sub layer is typically a clean sine wave at the fundamental frequency. Low-pass it heavily (below 100 Hz) to remove any harmonics. This is pure weight.

The Mid Layer

Character and definition. The mid layer can be a saw, square, or any bass synth with harmonic content. High-pass it around 80-120 Hz so it doesn't compete with the sub. This gives you note clarity.

Phase Alignment

Layers must be in phase. If the layers are out of phase, they cancel each other out. Check by inverting one layer's phase—if it sounds weaker, they were in phase. If it sounds stronger, flip the polarity.

Balance the Levels

Sub louder than mid. The sub layer should be the foundation. Bring up the mid layer just enough to hear the note clearly on laptop speakers. Too much mid makes it harsh; too little makes it disappear on small systems.

Pro Tips

  • Mono the sub: Keep sub layer centered (mono) for maximum punch
  • Widen the mid: Add subtle stereo width to the mid layer
  • Same MIDI: Both layers play the same notes for consistency
  • Test on phones: If you hear the bass on phone speakers, the mid layer is working
