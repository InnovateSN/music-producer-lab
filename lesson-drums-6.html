Step 1

What is syncopation?

Syncopation shifts emphasis from strong beats to unexpected positions between beats, creating movement and groove.

  • The kick doesn’t always land on the quarters: it can hit on the “&” or selected sixteenth notes.
  • Leaving space between hits increases rhythmic tension.
  • Listen and feel where the pattern “makes you move.”
Step 2

Kick pattern for this lesson.

The target for this lesson is a syncopated kick pattern (example): chosen steps (1, 3, 6, 11) in the 16-step grid.

  • In a 16-step grid: suggested target steps: 1, 3, 6, 11.
  • Snare: keep the backbeat on 5 and 13.
  • Hi-hat: eighths to maintain the pulse.
Exercise

Master Syncopated Kick Patterns

Break free from the 4-on-the-floor! Syncopation places kicks on unexpected beats, creating that groovy, off-beat feel found in funk, hip-hop, and many electronic genres.

What to do:

  1. Kick row: Steps 1, 3, 6, 11 - NOT on every beat!
  2. Snare row: Steps 5 and 13 (keep the backbeat steady).
  3. Hi-Hat row: Steps 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13, 15 (straight eighths).
  4. Notice how the kick on steps 3 and 6 creates tension against the snare.
Tip: Syncopation = surprise! The kick lands 'between' where you expect it. The steady snare/hi-hat anchors the groove while the kick adds movement.
