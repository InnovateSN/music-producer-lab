Sidechain & Ducking: Making Elements Breathe Together

Sidechain compression is one of the most powerful mixing techniques in modern music production. It allows one sound to control the compression applied to another—most commonly, the kick drum ducking the bass to create space for each hit. This technique is essential in EDM, hip-hop, and pop production, where the "pumping" rhythmic effect has become a defining sonic characteristic. In this lesson, you'll master sidechain compression setup, bass-to-kick ducking, the pumping effect, and ghost trigger techniques for creative applications.

How Sidechain Works

Normal compression reacts to its own input signal. Sidechain compression uses an external signal to trigger gain reduction. Place a compressor on the bass track, route the kick drum as the sidechain input. Now every time the kick hits, the bass ducks down by 3-6 dB, then returns. The bass never fights the kick for space—they take turns occupying the low-end frequency range.

Kick/Bass Ducking

The classic sidechain application: ducking bass to kick. Set the compressor's threshold so it triggers on every kick hit. Use a fast attack (0.1-1 ms) so ducking starts instantly. Set the release to match your tempo—fast enough that the bass returns before the next kick, slow enough to avoid clicking. A release of 100-200 ms works at most tempos. This creates a clean, punchy low end where kick and bass never overlap.

The Pumping Effect

When you exaggerate sidechain ducking on pads, synths, or even the entire mix bus, you get the iconic pumping effect heard in EDM and French house. Increase the ratio to 8:1 or higher, lower the threshold for deeper ducking (-10 to -20 dB), and set a medium release that creates a rhythmic swell. The music literally breathes with each beat, creating an infectious groove that drives the dancefloor.

Ghost Triggers & Creative Uses

A ghost trigger is a silent MIDI or audio track used solely to trigger sidechain ducking. This lets you create pumping effects even in sections without a kick drum, or with custom rhythmic patterns. Program any rhythm you want on the ghost track—triplets, syncopation, half-time—and the ducking follows that pattern. This unlocks creative rhythmic effects independent of your actual drum pattern.

Common Mistakes

Release Time Too Slow or Too Fast

If the release is too slow, the bass never fully returns before the next kick—you lose all low-end energy. If it's too fast, you hear an audible click or pop at the recovery point. The release must be tempo-matched: at 128 BPM, a quarter note is 469 ms, so your release should be roughly 200-400 ms to recover smoothly before the next hit.

Over-Ducking Everything

Sidechaining every single track to the kick creates an exaggerated pumping effect that sounds unnatural and fatiguing. Reserve heavy sidechain for bass and low-mid elements that actually compete with the kick. For pads and synths, use subtler ducking (2-3 dB) or apply it only to the low frequencies using a multiband compressor or EQ-based sidechain.

Not Using Sidechain When Needed

Some producers avoid sidechain because they think it's only for EDM. In reality, subtle sidechain ducking is used in virtually every genre to clean up kick/bass relationships. Even a gentle 2-3 dB duck with a fast release is invisible to the listener but makes the low end dramatically cleaner. If your kick sounds buried in the bass, sidechain is often the solution.

Why This Matters

Clean Low End — Sidechain ducking prevents kick and bass from competing, giving your mix a solid, punchy foundation.

Groove & Energy — The pumping effect adds rhythmic movement that makes listeners want to move and dance.

Loudness Without Clipping — By making room dynamically, sidechain lets you push overall levels higher without distortion.

Creative Rhythms — Ghost triggers open up unlimited rhythmic possibilities beyond simple four-on-the-floor patterns.
