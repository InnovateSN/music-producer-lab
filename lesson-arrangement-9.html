What You'll Learn

Layering Sounds Without Creating Mud

Layering is powerful—it creates full, rich sounds. But layering without frequency awareness creates mud: a cluttered, undefined mess where nothing stands out. The golden rule: one element per frequency range. Learn to layer strategically using frequency spacing, stereo separation, and careful EQ to build massive sounds that stay clean.

Frequency Spacing

Sub bass, bass, low-mids, mids, high-mids, highs—assign one dominant element to each range. If two elements occupy the same space, one must be EQ'd to make room for the other.

Stereo Separation

Spread layers across the stereo field. Lead vocal center, harmonies panned. Main synth centered, pad layers wide. Stereo width creates space even when frequencies overlap.

Subtractive EQ

Cut before you boost. High-pass everything that doesn't need low end. Cut competing frequencies rather than boosting to win. Subtraction creates clarity better than addition.

Less Is Often More

Before adding another layer, ask: "What's missing?" Sometimes one well-designed sound beats three mediocre layers. Complexity isn't quality.

Frequency Ranges

  • Sub bass (20-60Hz): 808, sub synth—one at a time
  • Bass (60-250Hz): Bass guitar, bass synth body
  • Low-mids (250-500Hz): Warmth zone—careful of mud
  • Mids (500Hz-2kHz): Vocals, main instruments
  • High-mids (2-6kHz): Presence, clarity, brightness
  • Highs (6kHz+): Air, sparkle, cymbals
