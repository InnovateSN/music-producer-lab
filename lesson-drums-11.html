Drum & Bass: The Fastest Beats in Electronic Music

Drum and Bass (DnB) is characterized by breakneck tempos (160-180 BPM), chopped breakbeats, and a signature "rolling" feel. Born in the UK rave scene of the early 1990s, DnB evolved from jungle and breakbeat hardcore. The legendary Amen break—a 6-second drum solo from "Amen, Brother" (1969)—became the foundation of the genre and remains one of the most sampled breaks in music history.

The Two-Step Groove

The classic DnB pattern uses a two-step rhythm: syncopated kicks with a snare on beats 2 and 4. At 174 BPM, this creates a half-time feel (perceived as 87 BPM), giving DnB its characteristic rolling, head-nodding groove despite the fast tempo.

The kick placement is crucial: kicks land between snare hits in syncopated patterns, creating forward momentum and energy. The result is simultaneously fast (from hi-hats and breaks) and groovy (from the half-time snare).

The Amen Break Legacy

The Amen break is a 6-second drum break that's been chopped, re-sequenced, and time-stretched in countless DnB, jungle, and hip-hop tracks. Producers take this break and manipulate it through:

  • Time-stretching: Speeding it up from original 136 BPM to 170+ BPM
  • Chopping: Slicing individual hits and rearranging them
  • Layering: Combining multiple breaks for complexity
  • Processing: EQ, compression, distortion for character

While you can program DnB drums from scratch (as in this lesson), understanding breakbeat manipulation is essential for authentic DnB production.

DnB Subgenres & Tempo

DnB encompasses many styles, each with distinct characteristics:

  • Liquid DnB (170-176 BPM): Melodic, soulful, smooth—jazzy chords and vocals
  • Jump Up (172-176 BPM): Aggressive, bouncy, heavy basslines
  • Neurofunk (174-178 BPM): Dark, technical, complex sound design
  • Jungle (160-170 BPM): Ragga vocals, chopped breaks, oldschool vibes

In this lesson, we use 174 BPM—the standard DnB tempo that gives that perfect balance of energy and groove.
