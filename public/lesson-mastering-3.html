Mastering EQ: Linear Phase & Mid-Side Processing

EQ in mastering is fundamentally different from mixing EQ. You're working on a complete stereo mix, so every adjustment affects everything. Mastering EQ requires subtle moves—often 0.5-2 dB—and specialized techniques. Linear phase EQ prevents phase smearing on transients. Mid-Side EQ lets you process the center and sides independently. These tools give you surgical precision for shaping the final tonal balance.

Linear Phase EQ

Standard EQ causes phase shift around boosted/cut frequencies—usually fine in mixing, but problematic on a full master. Linear phase EQ eliminates this, preserving transient clarity. Trade-off: increased latency and potential pre-ringing on sharp cuts. Use it for broad tonal shaping.

Mid-Side EQ

Mid = center (kick, snare, bass, vocals). Side = stereo information (room, width, effects). M/S EQ lets you brighten the sides without affecting vocals, or tighten bass in the center without touching stereo effects. Incredibly powerful for mastering.

Subtle is Key

Mastering EQ moves are typically 0.5-2 dB. If you need more than 3 dB, the problem is in the mix. Use wide Q values for gentle slopes. A/B frequently—if bypassing the EQ sounds better, remove it. Less is more; every change affects everything.

Common Mastering EQ Moves

High-pass at 20-30 Hz to remove sub rumble. Cut 200-400 Hz if muddy. Boost 2-4 kHz for presence. Shelf boost 10kHz+ for air. Cut 3-5 kHz if harsh. Always compare to reference tracks.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Heavy-Handed EQ: Boosting 6 dB in mastering is like shouting—it destroys balance. If you need dramatic EQ, the mix needs revision. Master EQ is refinement, not transformation.
Narrow Q Surgical Cuts: Tight Q notches work in mixing to fix individual tracks, but in mastering they create audible artifacts. Use broad, gentle curves—they sound more natural on a full mix.
Over-Boosting the Sides: M/S processing is powerful but dangerous. Too much side boost creates an artificial, hollow sound. Monitor in mono to check that center elements remain solid when you adjust sides.

Why This Matters

  • Tonal Balance: EQ is your primary tool for achieving commercial frequency balance that translates everywhere.
  • Phase Integrity: Linear phase preserves the punch and clarity that minimum-phase EQ can smear.
  • Surgical Control: M/S lets you fix problems in specific areas (like muddy center bass) without affecting the rest.
  • Reference Matching: EQ helps you match the tonal character of professional reference tracks.
