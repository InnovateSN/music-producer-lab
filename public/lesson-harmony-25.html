    Blues Harmony: 12-Bar & Turnarounds

    What You're Actually Making

    You are learning blues harmony — the 12-bar blues form and turnarounds that form the foundation of blues, rock, jazz, and R&B. The 12-bar blues is one of the most important chord progressions in modern music, with specific harmonic patterns that create the characteristic bluesy sound.

    Unlike typical diatonic progressions, blues uses dominant 7th chords on ALL chord degrees (I7, IV7, V7), not just V7. This creates unresolved tension throughout the progression, giving blues its distinctive sound.

    The 12-Bar Blues Form

    The basic 12-bar blues follows this structure in C major:

    Bars 1-4:  C7  | C7  | C7  | C7  |   (Tonic - I7)
Bars 5-6:  F7  | F7  |              (Subdominant - IV7)
Bars 7-8:  C7  | C7  |              (Back to tonic)
Bar 9:     G7  |                   (Dominant - V7)
Bar 10:    F7  |                   (Subdominant)
Bars 11-12: C7 | G7  ||            (Tonic + Turnaround)

    Why Dominant 7ths Throughout?

    • Creates bluesy, unresolved tension — all chords "want" to resolve but don't
    • Comes from the blues scale (flat 3, flat 7) applied to all chords
    • Standard in Delta blues, Chicago blues, and rock
    • The 7ths add color and prevent the progression from sounding too major or happy

    Chord Spelling in C Blues:

    C7:  C - E - G - Bb  (1 - 3 - 5 - b7)
F7:  F - A - C - Eb  (1 - 3 - 5 - b7)
G7:  G - B - D - F   (1 - 3 - 5 - b7)

    Quick-Change Blues

    The quick-change blues adds harmonic movement in bar 2 by going to IV7, then back to I7 in bar 3:

    Bars 1-4:  C7 | F7 | C7 | C7 |   (I7 - IV7 - I7 - I7)
Bars 5-6:  F7 | F7 |            (IV7 - IV7)
Bars 7-8:  C7 | C7 |            (I7 - I7)
Bar 9:     G7 |                 (V7)
Bar 10:    F7 |                 (IV7)
Bars 11-12: C7 | G7 ||          (I7 - V7 turnaround)

    Why Quick-Change?

    • More harmonic movement early in the progression
    • Common in modern blues, rock, and jump blues
    • Creates interest without complicating the overall form
    • Example: "Johnny B. Goode" (Chuck Berry)

    Jazz Blues

    Jazz blues adds ii-V substitutions and passing chords for sophistication. This is the bebop approach to blues:

    Bars 1-2:  Cmaj7 | F7       |   (I-IV7, or Imaj7-IV7)
Bars 3-4:  Cmaj7 | Cm7  F7  |   (I-ii7 V7/IV for bar 5)
Bars 5-6:  F7    | Fm7  Bb7 |   (IV7-iv7 bVII7)
Bars 7-8:  Cmaj7 | Em7  A7  |   (I-iii7 VI7, V/ii)
Bars 9-10: Dm7   | G7       |   (ii7-V7)
Bars 11-12: Cmaj7 A7 | Dm7 G7 || (I VI7 ii7 V7 turnaround)

    Key Features of Jazz Blues:

    • Major 7th on I chord: Cmaj7 instead of C7 for sophisticated tonic
    • ii-V substitutions: Bar 4 uses Cm7-F7 (ii-V/IV) to approach F7 in bar 5
    • Passing chords: Bar 6 uses Fm7-Bb7 (iv7-bVII7) for chromatic movement
    • Secondary dominants: Bar 8 uses A7 (V/ii) to tonicize Dm7 in bar 9
    • Example: "Blues for Alice" (Charlie Parker)

    Minor Blues

    Minor blues uses minor chords instead of dominant 7ths for a darker, more melancholic sound:

    Bars 1-4:  Cm7 | Cm7 | Cm7 | Cm7 |   (i7)
Bars 5-6:  Fm7 | Fm7 |              (iv7)
Bars 7-8:  Cm7 | Cm7 |              (i7)
Bar 9:     G7  |                   (V7 - still dominant!)
Bar 10:    Fm7 |                   (iv7)
Bars 11-12: Cm7 | G7  ||           (i7 - V7 turnaround)

    Key Differences:

    • Minor 7th chords on i and iv (Cm7, Fm7) instead of dominant 7ths
    • V7 remains dominant: G7 (not Gm7) for strong pull back to i
    • Darker, more emotional sound
    • Common in jazz blues and ballads
    • Example: "Mr. P.C." (John Coltrane)

    Turnarounds: Setting Up the Repeat

    Turnarounds are the last 2 bars (bars 11-12) that create harmonic motion back to bar 1. They're essential for making the blues loop smoothly.

    I-VI-ii-V Turnaround (Most Common):

    C7 - A7 - Dm7 - G7

Function: I → V/ii → ii → V
Bass line: C → A → D → G (descending then up)
Leads perfectly back to C7 in bar 1

    Chromatic Descent Turnaround (Bebop Style):

    C7 - Eb7 - Ab7 - Db7

Function: I → bIII7 → bVI7 → bII7 (all dominant 7ths)
Bass line: C → Eb → Ab → Db (descending by 4ths/5ths)
Very jazzy, chromatic sound

    I-Idim-ii-V Turnaround:

    C7 - C#dim7 - Dm7 - G7

Function: I → #Idim7 → ii → V
Chromatic passing chord (C#dim7) between I and ii
Creates smooth voice leading

    Rhythm Changes Turnaround:

    Cmaj7 - Am7 - Dm7 - G7

Function: I → vi → ii → V
From "I Got Rhythm" changes
Lighter, major sound (not bluesy)
Common in jazz standards

    Blues Substitutions and Advanced Techniques

    Bars 9-10 Substitutions:

    The standard bars 9-10 are G7-F7. You can substitute:

    • ii-V: Dm7-G7 (approaches I in bar 11 with ii-V motion)
    • Tritone subs: Db7-C7 (substituting G7→Db7, F7→C7 with tritone subs)
    • Tritone chain: D7-Ab7 (dominant 7ths descending by tritone)

    Bar 8 Substitutions (Before Bars 9-10):

    • V/ii approach: Em7-A7 (ii-V/ii, sets up Dm7 in bar 9)
    • V/IV approach: Gm7-C7 (ii-V/IV, prepares F7 in bar 10)

    Bird Blues (Charlie Parker Style):

    | Cmaj7  F7   | Bb7  A7   | Dm7  G7   | Em7  A7   |
| Dm7         | G7        | Cmaj7     | Em7  A7   |
| Dm7         | G7        | Cmaj7  A7 | Dm7  G7   ||

    Very sophisticated bebop blues with rapid harmonic rhythm and ii-V substitutions everywhere.

    Genre-Specific Blues Applications

    Delta Blues:

    • Basic 12-bar, simple I-IV-V (often just I7-IV7-V7)
    • Acoustic guitar, slide guitar, minimal instrumentation
    • Example: Robert Johnson, Son House
    • Emphasis on feel and phrasing over harmonic complexity

    Chicago Blues:

    • Electric guitar, walking bass, harmonica
    • Quick-change blues common
    • Shuffle rhythm, triplet feel
    • Example: Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf, Buddy Guy

    Jazz Blues:

    • Complex substitutions, ii-V's, passing chords
    • Walking bass, swing feel
    • Sophisticated turnarounds
    • Example: "Blues for Alice" (Parker), "Billie's Bounce", "Tenor Madness"

    Rock Blues:

    • Simplified 12-bar, power chords, distortion
    • Often omits 7ths (uses I-IV-V instead of I7-IV7-V7)
    • Example: "Red House" (Jimi Hendrix), ZZ Top, Stevie Ray Vaughan

    R&B/Soul Blues:

    • Extended chords (9ths, 13ths) on I, IV, V
    • Slower tempos, emotional delivery
    • Example: Ray Charles, B.B. King ballads, Bobby "Blue" Bland

    Walking Bass in Blues

    Blues bass lines typically walk between chord tones using stepwise motion. This creates forward momentum:

    C7 bass line (1 bar):  C - E - G - Bb   (root - 3 - 5 - b7)
                       or
                       C - G - Bb - B   (root - 5 - b7 - leading tone)

F7 bass line (1 bar):  F - A - C - Eb   (root - 3 - 5 - b7)

G7 bass line (1 bar):  G - B - D - F    (root - 3 - 5 - b7)

    The walking bass creates rhythmic and harmonic continuity, essential for blues groove.

    Blues Scale Integration

    The blues scale works over the entire 12-bar blues progression:

    C blues scale: C - Eb - F - F# - G - Bb - C
               1 - b3 - 4 - #4 - 5 - b7 - 1

    Blue Notes (Key Tensions):

    • Flat 3rd (Eb): Creates tension against major 3rd (E) in C7 chord
    • Flat 7th (Bb): Reinforces the dominant 7th quality of all chords
    • Sharp 4th/Flat 5th (F#): Maximum tension, the quintessential "blues note"

    The blues scale works because it emphasizes the chromatic notes that define blues harmony — especially the b3, b7, and #4.

    Common Mistakes to Avoid

    • Using major or minor triads: Blues requires dominant 7ths (I7, IV7, V7). Using C major or Cm instead of C7 loses the blues character entirely.
    • Forgetting the turnaround: Bars 11-12 (I7-V7 or I-VI-ii-V) are essential for looping. Without them, the progression feels incomplete.
    • Playing melody that clashes with IV7: Many melodies that work over I7 and V7 don't work over IV7 (bar 5). Be aware of the chord change.
    • Not swinging or shuffling: Blues uses triplet feel (swing/shuffle) in most styles. Straight 8ths sound wrong unless you're doing rock blues.
    • Ignoring the form: 12 bars must be exact. Adding or removing bars breaks the blues form and confuses other musicians.

    Why This Matters

    The 12-bar blues is one of the most important progressions in modern music because it:

    • Forms the foundation of multiple genres: Blues, rock, jazz, R&B, soul, and country all use blues harmony
    • Provides a universal jam structure: Musicians worldwide know the 12-bar blues, making it perfect for improvisation and sessions
    • Teaches harmonic tension and release: The dominant 7th chords and turnarounds demonstrate how to create and resolve harmonic tension
    • Enables improvisation: The predictable structure allows soloists to improvise freely while staying harmonically grounded
    • Serves as a template for thousands of songs: "Sweet Home Chicago", "Route 66", "Johnny B. Goode", "Crossroads", "The Thrill Is Gone", "Red House", and countless jazz standards

    Mastering blues harmony gives you access to over a century of American music. Whether you're producing blues, rock, jazz, or R&B, understanding the 12-bar blues form and turnarounds is essential.
