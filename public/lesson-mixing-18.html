Mixing for Streaming: Loudness Normalization Changes Everything

Streaming platforms like Spotify, Apple Music, and YouTube use loudness normalization—they automatically adjust playback volume so all songs play at a consistent loudness. This fundamentally changes the "loudness war": over-limiting your master to be loud now hurts your sound, because the platform turns it down anyway while you've already destroyed your dynamic range. Understanding LUFS targets and how to optimize for each platform is essential for modern music production and release.

LUFS Explained

LUFS (Loudness Units Full Scale) is the industry standard for measuring perceived loudness integrated over time. Unlike peak dBFS, LUFS reflects how loud music actually sounds to human ears. Streaming platforms measure your track's integrated LUFS and apply gain to bring it to their target—no matter what you do at the limiter.

Platform Targets

Spotify: -14 LUFS integrated. Apple Music: -16 LUFS (Soundcheck). YouTube: -13 to -15 LUFS. Tidal: -14 LUFS. SoundCloud: -14 LUFS. Louder than the target = turned down. Quieter = turned up. Target -14 LUFS for maximum compatibility across platforms.

Dynamic Range Strategy

Since platforms normalize loudness, dynamic range becomes your competitive advantage. A mix with -14 LUFS integrated and plenty of dynamic range sounds richer and more professional than one squashed to -8 LUFS that gets turned down. Target -14 LUFS with minimal limiting—use limiting only to control true peaks, not to maximize loudness.

Codec Considerations

Streaming uses lossy compression: Spotify 320kbps OGG, Apple Music 256kbps AAC, YouTube 128kbps AAC. True peaks close to 0 dBFS can clip after codec encoding (inter-sample peaks). Limit true peaks to -1 dBTP (some engineers use -0.5 dBTP) to prevent distortion after encoding.

Common Mistakes

Over-Limiting to "Win" the Loudness War

Pushing your master to -8 or -6 LUFS integrated does not make it louder on streaming platforms—it makes it quieter (turned down to -14) while destroying transients, dynamics, and punch. The loudness war is over: if you over-limit, you lose dynamics for no gain. Trust the LUFS meter, not your feeling that louder = better.

Not Checking True Peak Levels

Standard peak meters miss inter-sample peaks—moments between samples that can clip after digital-to-analog conversion or codec encoding. A mix peaking at -0.1 dBFS might have true peaks at +0.3 dBTP, causing distortion on streaming platforms. Use a true peak limiter and target -1 dBTP maximum on your final master. iZotope Ozone, FabFilter Pro-L 2, and most limiters have true peak mode.

Submitting at Wrong Sample Rate or Bit Depth

Streaming platforms accept specific formats. Submitting a 22kHz or 32-bit float file when the platform expects 44.1kHz/16-bit WAV can cause encoding artifacts. The safest universal delivery format is WAV, 44.1kHz or 48kHz, 24-bit. Let the distributor handle conversion to the platform's native format. Never submit MP3 or lossy formats for upload.

Why This Matters

Compete professionally — Knowing streaming loudness targets means your release sounds as powerful as major label releases, not louder but destroyed or quieter and weak. You're on a level playing field.

Preserve your dynamics — Understanding normalization lets you stop limiting aggressively and start preserving the punchy, dynamic mixes you worked hard to create. Dynamic range IS the prize.

Consistent experience — Properly targeted masters sound consistent when auto-played between tracks on playlists. Badly targeted tracks sound jarringly quiet or distorted—a bad first impression for new listeners.

Professional delivery — Labels, sync clients, and distributors specify LUFS targets and true peak limits in their specs. Knowing this makes you immediately more professional and deliverable-ready.
