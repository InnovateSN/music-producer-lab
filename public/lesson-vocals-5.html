Vocal Chain Essentials: EQ, Compression & De-Essing

The vocal processing chain transforms a raw recording into a polished, radio-ready vocal. Every professional vocal goes through a series of processors: EQ for tone shaping, compression for dynamic control, de-essing for harsh sibilance, and often saturation for warmth. The order and settings define your vocal's character.

EQ (Equalization)

Shape the tone. High-pass around 80-100 Hz to remove rumble. Cut muddy frequencies (200-400 Hz). Boost presence (3-5 kHz) for clarity. Add air (10-12 kHz) for sparkle.

Compression

Control dynamics. Even out loud and quiet parts. Start with 4:1 ratio, medium attack (10-30ms), fast release (50-100ms). Aim for 3-6 dB gain reduction on peaks. Keeps vocal upfront.

De-Essing

Tame harsh "S" sounds. Sibilance (4-8 kHz) can pierce through a mix. A de-esser compresses only when sibilance occurs. Set threshold so only harsh S/T sounds trigger reduction.

Saturation

Add warmth and presence. Subtle saturation adds harmonics that help vocals cut through the mix. Tape saturation is warm; tube saturation is aggressive. Use sparingly—a little goes a long way.

Common Mistakes

Too Many Plugins in the Chain

Adding plugin after plugin creates compounding coloration and phase issues. Each plugin should serve a specific, deliberate purpose. Start with three essentials: subtractive EQ, compressor, de-esser. Add more only when you identify a specific remaining problem. Most professional vocal chains use 4-6 plugins maximum—less is almost always more.

Wrong Plugin Order

Plugin order significantly changes the result. Putting a compressor before subtractive EQ means the compressor reacts to problem frequencies you'll later cut—causing more gain reduction than intended on those bands. The standard order is subtractive EQ first, then compression, then de-essing, then additive EQ. Saturation and character go at the end where they color the already-shaped signal.

Not Gain-Staging Between Plugins

When EQ boosts or compression adds makeup gain, the input level of the next plugin changes. A plugin hitting too hot applies more saturation or distortion than intended; hitting too quiet misses the plugin's optimal operating range. Match output levels between plugins—each stage should pass signal at roughly the same RMS level it received. Use the output gain control on each plugin to calibrate the handoff.

Why This Matters

Your chain is your signature sound — The specific combination of plugins and their ordering defines the sonic character you bring to every project. Developing a reliable chain means every vocal you touch carries your professional fingerprint.

Consistent professional results — A proven chain applied to well-recorded vocals produces reliable professional results every session, rather than starting from scratch and guessing. Reliability is what clients pay for and studios hire for.

Signal flow understanding — Knowing why plugin order matters and what each stage does to the signal transfers to every mixing context. It builds the foundation for mixing any source in any genre with the same systematic approach.

Templates save time — Once you've built a chain that works, save it as a DAW template. Starting every vocal track with your proven chain saves setup time and lets you focus on musical decisions from the very first note of the session.
