Mid-Side Processing: Surgical Stereo Control

Mid-Side (M/S) processing splits a stereo signal into two independent components: the Mid (center information: kick, bass, lead vocal) and the Side (stereo information: guitars, pads, ambience). By processing each independently, you gain surgical control over the stereo field—tightening the center without affecting width, widening the sides without muddying the center, and fixing mono compatibility issues that normal stereo processing can't touch. M/S is a mastering standard and an increasingly essential mixing technique.

M/S Fundamentals

Mid = Left + Right (sum — mono center information). Side = Left - Right (difference — stereo information). After processing, it decodes back to stereo. Mid and Side are mathematically independent, allowing separate processing of center vs. stereo content without affecting each other.

M/S EQ Moves

Classic M/S EQ: High-pass Mid at 30-40 Hz (remove sub rumble from center). High-pass Side at 200-300 Hz (tighten stereo low-end). Boost Side 10-12 kHz (+2 to +4dB for air and width). Cut Mid 200-400 Hz (-1 to -3dB if center is muddy). All of this without touching the other component.

M/S Compression

Center elements (kick, bass, vocal) need consistent dynamics: compress Mid harder (3:1 to 6:1, moderate attack). Stereo content (pads, guitars) benefits from lighter compression: compress Side at 2:1 to 4:1 with slower attack. This maintains transient clarity in the stereo field while controlling center dynamics.

Safe Stereo Widening

Keep lows narrow/mono (0-20% width at 20-150 Hz), widen mids moderately (40-60% at 150 Hz-5 kHz), widen highs aggressively (70-100% at 5-20 kHz). Always test mono compatibility after widening: sum to mono and listen for elements that disappear or thin out — that's phase cancellation requiring correction.

Common Mistakes

Not Checking Mono Compatibility

Every stereo widening technique—Haas delay, M/S side boost, stereo synthesizers—can cause phase cancellation in mono. Many listeners use mono speakers (phones, smart speakers, earbuds with shared sub). Always check mono after any stereo processing. If elements disappear or thin out, reduce the widening or fix the phase before declaring the mix complete.

Over-Widening the Stereo Image

A super-wide mix sounds exciting in the sweet spot of a studio but collapses in mono and sounds hollow on small speakers. Professional mixes have controlled, strategic width—not maximum width everywhere. Narrow the low frequencies completely (sub and bass must be mono), keep mids moderate, and reserve maximum width for high-frequency elements like air and shimmer.

Applying M/S Without Soloing Mid and Side

M/S EQ and compression should be set with Mid and Side soloed separately before listening to the full stereo result. Listen to Mid solo: identify problems in center (muddiness, harshness, level issues). Listen to Side solo: identify problems in stereo field (imbalance, noise, harsh highs). Process each, then listen full to verify the combination achieves what you intended.

Why This Matters

Surgical precision — Fix muddy center without narrowing the mix. Add air to the sides without brightening the vocal. M/S gives you two independent channels where standard stereo gives you one blend.

Mastering-grade technique — M/S is used on virtually every professional mastering chain. Learning it in the mix stage gives you mastering-level control without needing a separate mastering session for stereo adjustments.

Mix translation — Controlling center vs. sides independently ensures your mix translates from studio monitors to earbuds, club systems, and mono speakers without losing critical elements.

Fix problem mixes — Harsh vocal in the center? Too much low-end in the stereo field? M/S lets you fix these problems without EQ-ing everything else in the mix. It's the most surgical problem-solving tool available to mixers.
