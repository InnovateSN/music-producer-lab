    Funk & Soul Harmony: Groovy Sophisticated Chords

    What You're Actually Making

    You are learning funk and soul harmony — the extended chords, sus chords, slash chords, and modal vamps that create the groovy, sophisticated sound of funk, soul, and R&B. Unlike simpler rock or pop harmony, funk uses rich chord voicings (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) and emphasizes rhythm as much as harmony.

    In funk, chords are rhythmic instruments. The emphasis is on 16th-note stabs, syncopation, ghost notes, and groove. Harmony serves the rhythm, not the other way around.

    Extended Minor Chords

    Funk and soul love extended minor chords — especially minor 9ths and minor 11ths:

    Dm9 (Minor 9th):

    Notes: D - F - A - C - E
Formula: 1 - b3 - 5 - b7 - 9
Sound: Rich, sophisticated, mellow
Usage: Tonic or ii chord in funk progressions

    Dm11 (Minor 11th):

    Notes: D - F - A - C - E - G
Formula: 1 - b3 - 5 - b7 - 9 - 11
Sound: Very lush, open, floating
Usage: Modal vamps (Dorian mode), one-chord funk
Example: "Chameleon" (Herbie Hancock) uses Dm11 vamp

    Voicing Tip: When voicing extended minor chords on keyboard, omit the 5th to avoid muddiness. Play root in left hand, 3-7-9 or 7-9-11 in right hand.

    Sus Chords: Tension Without Resolution

    Sus (suspended) chords replace the 3rd with the 4th, creating an unresolved, floating sound:

    Gsus4 (Basic Suspended 4th):

    Notes: G - C - D
Formula: 1 - 4 - 5 (no 3rd!)
Sound: Open, unresolved, tense
Resolution: Usually resolves to G major (C→B) or Gm (C→Bb)

    G7sus4 (Dominant 7th Suspended):

    Notes: G - C - D - F
Formula: 1 - 4 - 5 - b7
Sound: Funky, groovy, tense dominant
Usage: Creates dominant tension without major/minor commitment
Example: Resolves to G7, or stays unresolved for funk vamp

    G9sus4 (9th Suspended):

    Notes: G - C - D - F - A
Formula: 1 - 4 - 5 - b7 - 9
Sound: Very sophisticated, modern funk
Usage: Tower of Power, Stevie Wonder style

    Why Sus Chords Work in Funk:

    • Creates harmonic tension that drives groove forward
    • The 4th creates dissonance that "wants" to resolve but doesn't have to
    • Open, ambiguous sound lets the melody and rhythm shine
    • Can vamp on sus4 chord for extended sections without sounding static

    Slash Chords: Smooth Bass Lines & Polychords

    Slash chords specify a triad/chord in the right hand and a specific bass note in the left. They create smooth bass lines and modern harmony:

    C/E (C Major over E Bass):

    Right hand: C major triad (C-E-G)
Left hand: E bass
Result: C major in first inversion
Sound: Smooth, less root-heavy
Usage: Creates stepwise bass lines

    Dm7/G (Dm7 over G Bass):

    Right hand: Dm7 (D-F-A-C)
Left hand: G bass
Result: G9sus sound (G-D-F-A-C = G-2-b7-9-4)
Sound: Modern, sophisticated
Usage: V chord substitute, gospel/neo-soul

    F/G (F Major over G Bass):

    Right hand: F major triad (F-A-C)
Left hand: G bass
Result: G9sus (G-F-A-C = 1-b7-9-11)
Sound: Floating, unresolved dominant
Usage: Funk/soul V substitute, modal harmony

    Common Slash Chord Bass Line:

    C/E - F/A - G/B - C

Bass line: E - A - B - C (stepwise ascent)
Harmonically: I/3 - IV/6 - V/3 - I
Creates smooth, modern progression

    Dominant Chords with Extensions

    Funk uses extended dominant chords for color and tension:

    G13 (Dominant 13th):

    Notes: G - B - F - A - E (omit 5th and 11th)
Formula: 1 - 3 - b7 - 9 - 13
Sound: Sophisticated, jazzy funk
Usage: V7 substitute, Tower of Power style

    G7#9 ("Hendrix Chord"):

    Notes: G - B - F - D# (Bb enharmonic)
Formula: 1 - 3 - b7 - #9
Sound: Bluesy, aggressive, psychedelic
Usage: Purple Haze, funk-rock, blues-rock

    G7b9 (Dominant Flat 9th):

    Notes: G - B - F - Ab
Formula: 1 - 3 - b7 - b9
Sound: Tense, bebop, sophisticated
Usage: Jazz-funk, bebop-influenced soul

    Modal Harmony: Dorian and Mixolydian Vamps

    Funk often uses modal vamps — staying on one chord for extended sections while improvising over a mode:

    Dorian Vamp (Minor Funk):

    Chord: Dm9 or Dm11
Scale: D Dorian (D-E-F-G-A-B-C)
Key difference from natural minor: Raised 6th (B natural)
Sound: Funky minor, not sad
Example: "So What" (Miles Davis), Dorian vamps in soul

    Mixolydian Groove (Dominant Funk):

    Chord: G7 or G9
Scale: G Mixolydian (G-A-B-C-D-E-F)
Key difference from major: Flat 7th (F natural)
Sound: Bluesy major, funky
Example: "Superstition" (Stevie Wonder)

    Why Modal Vamps Work:

    • Minimal harmonic change = maximum groove focus
    • Allows rhythm section to lock in perfectly
    • Creates hypnotic, trance-like funk feel
    • Melody and improvisation provide interest, not chord changes

    Common Funk & Soul Progressions

    1. ii-V-I Funk (Extended Chords):

    Dm9 - G13 - Cmaj9

Play with 16th-note rhythm, ghost notes
Example: Tower of Power, Earth Wind & Fire

    2. Modal Funk (One-Chord Vamp):

    Dm11 (vamp for 8-16 bars)

Minimal harmony, maximum groove
Example: "Chameleon" (Herbie Hancock)

    3. Chromatic Funk (Chord Slides):

    Dm9 - Ebm9 - Em9 - F#m9

Chromatic ascent of minor 9th chords
Example: "Pick Up the Pieces" (Average White Band)

    4. Soul Progression (Extended I-vi-ii-V):

    Cmaj9 - Am9 - Dm9 - G13

Extended version of doo-wop changes
Example: Motown, Stax Records

    5. Gospel Changes (Slash Chords):

    C - F/A - Dm7 - G7

or: I - IV/vi - ii7 - V7

Heavy use of slash chords for smooth bass
Example: Kirk Franklin, Aretha Franklin

    Rhythm Techniques: 16th-Notes, Ghost Notes, and Syncopation

    In funk, how you play chords matters more than which chords you play:

    16th-Note Stabs:

    • Play chords on the AND of beats (off-beats)
    • Creates syncopated, funky feel
    • Example: "Le Freak" (Chic), "Brick House" (Commodores)

    Ghost Notes:

    • Muted chords between main hits
    • Adds percussive, rhythmic texture
    • Essential for funk guitar (mute strings with palm)

    Chord Hits on "One":

    • Accent beat 1 heavily, then ghost notes on 16ths
    • Creates rhythmic anchor for the groove
    • Example: James Brown, Parliament-Funkadelic

    Open Voicings:

    • Spread chord across 2+ octaves
    • Use slash chords for smooth bass movement
    • Prevents muddiness in dense funk arrangements

    Rootless Voicings (Piano/Keys):

    • Play 3-7-9 in right hand, root in left
    • Or 7-9-3 ("So What" voicing from Miles Davis)
    • Modern, open sound

    Genre-Specific Applications

    Classic Funk (1970s):

    • Extended minor chords (Dm9, Dm11), sus chords (G7sus4)
    • Chromatic movement, modal vamps
    • 16th-note rhythm, tight groove
    • Example: Earth Wind & Fire, Kool & the Gang, Ohio Players

    Neo-Soul (2000s):

    • Heavy use of maj9, min11, 13ths
    • Slash chords for smooth bass lines
    • J Dilla-influenced rhythm (loose, behind the beat)
    • Example: D'Angelo, Erykah Badu, H.E.R., Robert Glasper

    Modern R&B:

    • Extended chords, chromatic passing chords
    • Trap-influenced rhythm (slower, more spacious)
    • Alt-R&B uses dissonant extensions (#9, b13)
    • Example: The Weeknd, SZA, Frank Ocean

    Gospel:

    • Slash chords, suspensions resolving to major
    • Rhythmically dense, call-and-response
    • Heavy use of Dm7/G (G9sus) and F/G
    • Example: Kirk Franklin, Tye Tribbett, Marvin Sapp

    Jazz-Funk:

    • Modal vamps, complex extensions (13ths, alterations)
    • Virtuosic solos over simple harmony
    • Syncopated comping, walking bass
    • Example: Herbie Hancock, Tower of Power, Snarky Puppy

    Common Mistakes to Avoid

    • Playing full chords (too dense, muddy): Funk voicings should be open and spread out. Omit the 5th, use rootless voicings, avoid stacking notes too close together.
    • Not syncopating rhythm (loses funk feel): Playing chords on downbeats only sounds stiff. Funk lives on the 16th-note AND of beats — play off-beat!
    • Ignoring ghost notes (sounds stiff): Ghost notes add percussive groove. Without them, funk sounds mechanical and lifeless.
    • Over-complicating harmony (groove > chords): Funk is about groove first, harmony second. A simple Dm9 with perfect rhythm beats complex jazz chords with stiff rhythm every time.
    • Playing on downbeats only (not funky!): The "one" is important, but the magic happens between the beats. Syncopation is essential.

    Why This Matters

    Funk and soul harmony techniques are essential for modern music production because they:

    • Add sophistication to chord progressions: Extended chords (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) make simple progressions sound professional and rich
    • Create rhythmic interest: Funk teaches you that chords are rhythmic instruments, not just harmonic building blocks
    • Bridge multiple genres: Funk harmony appears in R&B, soul, neo-soul, gospel, jazz-funk, and modern hip-hop/pop production
    • Enable smooth bass lines: Slash chords create stepwise bass motion, essential for groove-oriented music
    • Provide tools for modal composition: Modal vamps (Dorian, Mixolydian) offer an alternative to traditional chord progressions

    Understanding funk harmony gives you the vocabulary to produce sophisticated, groove-oriented music. Whether you're making neo-soul, R&B, gospel, or jazz-funk, these techniques are foundational.
