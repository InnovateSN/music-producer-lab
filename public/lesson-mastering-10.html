Masterclass: Master a Complete Album or EP

Mastering an album or EP is different from mastering a single track. The challenge is creating sonic cohesion—all tracks should feel like they belong together while maintaining their individual character. This involves consistent loudness, balanced tonal character, proper spacing, and careful sequencing. This masterclass walks through the complete album mastering workflow from start to delivery.

Album Cohesion

All tracks should share a tonal family—similar brightness, low-end weight, and stereo width. Match perceived loudness so no track jumps out. Create a "template" from the best-mixed track and reference it for others. The album should feel like a journey, not a compilation.

Album Mastering Workflow

1) Listen to all tracks, identify the "hero" track. 2) Master the hero track first—this sets the standard. 3) Master remaining tracks, referencing the hero. 4) Level-match all tracks. 5) Listen to the album front-to-back. 6) Make final adjustments. 7) Set spacing and export.

Track Spacing & Sequencing

Gap between tracks: typically 2-4 seconds, but vary by transition. Crossfades for continuous flow. Fast song to slow? Longer gap. Energy matching? Shorter gap. Sequencing affects the listener's emotional journey. CD-TEXT, ISRC codes, and metadata are part of the delivery.

Final Delivery

Deliver: Individual WAV masters (for streaming), DDP image (for CD manufacturing), production master with spacing. Include metadata: ISRC codes, track names, artists. Provide different versions if needed (clean/explicit, radio edits). Document your processing.

Album Mastering Workflow: 6 Steps

1
Listen to Everything

Listen to all tracks in album order. Identify the best-mixed track (your "hero"). Note tonal differences, loudness variations, and problem areas.

2
Master the Hero Track

Apply your full mastering chain to the hero track. This sets the standard for loudness, tone, and width for the whole album.

3
Master Remaining Tracks

Process each remaining track, constantly referencing the hero. Adjust EQ and processing to match the tonal family while respecting each song's character.

4
Level-Match & Sequence

Match perceived loudness across all tracks. Set track spacing (typically 2-4 seconds). Consider emotional flow and energy arc.

5
Full Listen & Revise

Play the entire album front-to-back without stopping. Note any transitions that feel off, loudness jumps, or tonal inconsistencies. Make corrections.

6
Export & Deliver

Export individual WAVs for streaming, DDP for CD. Include ISRC codes, metadata. Provide different versions if needed (clean/explicit, radio edits).

Album Delivery Checklist

Technical Requirements

  • Individual WAV files (16-bit/44.1 kHz or 24-bit)
  • True peak at -1 dBTP or lower
  • Consistent loudness across all tracks
  • DDP image for CD replication (if applicable)

Metadata & Documentation

  • ISRC codes embedded per track
  • Track names, artist, album title
  • Processing notes (chain, settings, rationale)
  • Clean/explicit versions, radio edits (if needed)

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mastering Tracks in Isolation: Each track sounds great alone but they don't fit together. Always reference against other album tracks, not just commercial releases.
Inconsistent Loudness: One track being 2 LUFS louder breaks the album flow. Match integrated loudness carefully. Use the quietest track as your reference point.
Ignoring the Full Listen: You must listen to the entire album start-to-finish at least once before delivering. Issues in transitions and consistency only reveal themselves in context.

Why This Matters

  • Professional Standards: Albums from major labels have consistent, cohesive masters. Match that quality.
  • Listener Experience: A well-mastered album takes the listener on a journey without jarring transitions.
  • Client Reputation: Your mastering reflects on the artist. Deliver work they'll be proud of.
  • Career Building: Album mastering credits carry weight. Do it right and build your reputation.
