What You'll Learn

Intervals: The DNA of Music

An interval is the distance between two notes. Every melody, chord, and harmony is built from intervals — they are the fundamental building blocks of all music. Understanding intervals lets you analyze songs, build chords, create melodies, and communicate with other musicians using a universal language. A half step (semitone) is the smallest interval on a piano — one key to the very next key. A whole step (tone) equals two half steps. In your DAW, every note in your MIDI piano roll is separated by half steps.

Half Steps & Whole Steps

The Two Smallest Units

Half step: C to C#, E to F (adjacent keys on the keyboard). Whole step: C to D, E to F# (skip one key). These two units build every scale, chord, and melody in Western music. There are 12 half steps in one octave.

Why it matters: Every interval is measured in half steps. Learn to count them and you can identify any interval instantly.

Interval Names & Qualities

Number + Quality System

Intervals have a number (2nd, 3rd, 4th...) and a quality (major, minor, perfect, augmented, diminished). A major 3rd = 4 half steps. A perfect 5th = 7 half steps. Perfect intervals: unison, 4th, 5th, octave.

Why it matters: This naming system lets you communicate precisely about pitch relationships with any musician worldwide.

Interval Sound & Character

Hear the Emotion

Each interval has a distinct emotional character. Minor 2nd: tense, dissonant (Jaws theme). Major 3rd: bright, happy. Perfect 5th: powerful, open (Star Wars). Octave: same note, higher register.

Why it matters: Recognizing intervals by ear lets you transcribe melodies, identify chords, and write music faster.

Intervals Build Everything

Foundation of All Theory

Chords = stacked intervals (usually 3rds). Scales = specific interval patterns. Melodies = interval sequences. Master intervals and everything else in music theory clicks into place.

Why it matters: This is lesson 1 for a reason. Every concept that follows depends on understanding intervals.

Piano Keyboard: Natural Half Steps

Most adjacent keys have a black key between them (C-C#-D). But there are two places on the keyboard where white keys are directly adjacent with no black key between them:

E to F = Half Step

No black key between E and F. These white keys are natural half steps.

B to C = Half Step

No black key between B and C. The other natural half step on the keyboard.

Remembering E-F and B-C as natural half steps is essential — it explains why scales and keys work the way they do.

Common Mistakes with Intervals

Forgetting E-F and B-C Are Natural Half Steps

Students assume every white key has a black key next to it. But E-F and B-C are adjacent white keys with no black key between them — they are already half steps.

Fix: Memorize the two natural half steps (E-F, B-C). Look at a piano keyboard — you can see the gaps where black keys are missing.

Confusing Interval Number with Interval Quality

A "3rd" is the number (distance of three letter names). But C-E (major 3rd) and C-Eb (minor 3rd) are both 3rds — they differ in quality. The number tells you how many letters apart; the quality tells you the exact half-step count.

Fix: Always specify both the quality AND the number: "major 3rd" not just "a 3rd." Count the half steps to confirm quality.

Not Counting Inclusively for Interval Numbers

To name an interval number, you count the starting note as "1." C to E: C(1) - D(2) - E(3) = a 3rd. Beginners sometimes start counting from the second note and get the wrong number.

Fix: Always include the starting note in your count. C to G: C(1)-D(2)-E(3)-F(4)-G(5) = a 5th.

Ignoring Enharmonic Equivalents

C# and Db are the same pitch on a piano but are different interval names. C to C# is an augmented unison; C to Db is a minor 2nd. In production, they sound identical, but in theory, the spelling matters.

Fix: For production purposes, focus on the sound (half-step count). For theory exams, learn proper enharmonic spelling.

Why Intervals Change Everything for Producers

  • Build Chords from Scratch: A major chord = root + major 3rd + perfect 5th. A minor chord = root + minor 3rd + perfect 5th. Know intervals, build any chord without looking it up.
  • Transpose Any Melody: If you know a melody is "up a major 3rd, down a minor 2nd, up a perfect 5th," you can start it on any note and recreate it perfectly.
  • Create Emotional Impact: Minor intervals sound sad and dark. Major intervals sound bright and happy. Tritones sound tense and unstable. Use intervals deliberately to control the mood of your music.
  • Communicate with Collaborators: "Move that vocal up a minor 3rd" is precise and instant. Without interval knowledge, you'd say "make it a bit higher" and waste time guessing.
  • Unlock All Future Lessons: Scales, modes, chords, harmony, voice leading — every concept in this course builds on intervals. This lesson is your foundation.
