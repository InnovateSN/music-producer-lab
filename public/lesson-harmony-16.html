Suspended Chords: Tension, Resolution, and Voice Leading

What You're Actually Making

You are building suspended chords (sus2 and sus4) that resolve to major or minor chords — creating smooth, emotionally satisfying harmonic movement. This is the foundation of neo-soul, jazz, and R&B keyboard playing (Robert Glasper, Hiatus Kaiyote, Cory Henry).

Suspended chords create gentle tension because they lack the third (the note that defines major/minor). When the third finally appears, it creates resolution — a deeply satisfying musical moment.

What Are Suspended Chords?

A suspended chord (sus) is a chord where the third is replaced by either the 2nd (sus2) or the 4th (sus4).

Normal major chord structure: Root - 3rd - 5th

E major: E - G# - B
         ↑   ↑    ↑
      root 3rd  5th

Suspended chord variations:

Esus2:  E - F# - B   (2nd replaces the 3rd)
        ↑   ↑    ↑
     root  2nd  5th (no 3rd!)

Esus4:  E - A - B    (4th replaces the 3rd)
        ↑   ↑   ↑
     root  4th 5th (no 3rd!)

Why "suspended"? The term comes from classical music — the non-chord tone (2nd or 4th) is "suspended" in the air, waiting to resolve down or up to the third.

Why Suspended Chords Create Tension

The third defines whether a chord is major or minor:

  • Major third (4 semitones): E - G# = major chord (bright)
  • Minor third (3 semitones): E - G = minor chord (dark)

When you remove the third, the chord loses its identity:

  • Esus2 or Esus4: Neither major nor minor — it's ambiguous
  • This ambiguity creates a floating, unresolved quality
  • Our ears want the third to appear and resolve the tension

The tension is gentle, not harsh:

  • Sus chords are consonant (not dissonant) — they don't clash
  • The tension is emotional, not physical — creates anticipation, not pain
  • This makes them perfect for dreamy, atmospheric, or introspective music

How Suspended Chords Resolve

Resolution happens when the suspended note (2nd or 4th) moves to the 3rd:

Example 1: Esus2 → E major

Esus2:  E - F# - B   (tension — no 3rd)
        ↓    ↓
E major: E - G# - B   (resolution — F# moves up to G#)

Example 2: Esus4 → E major

Esus4:   E - A - B   (tension — no 3rd)
         ↓   ↓
E major: E - G# - B   (resolution — A moves down to G#)

Example 3: Asus4 → Amaj7

Asus4:  A - D - E       (tension)
        ↓   ↓
Amaj7:  A - C# - E - G#  (full resolution with 7th for smoothness)

Why this feels satisfying: The single-note motion (F#→G# or A→G#) is smooth and natural. This is called voice leading — minimizing movement between chords.

Voice Leading: The Secret to Smooth Progressions

Voice leading is the art of moving from one chord to another with minimal note movement. Good voice leading sounds smooth and connected, not jumpy or awkward.

Example of good voice leading (Esus2 → E):

Esus2:  E - F# - B
         ↓   ↓    ↓
E major: E - G# - B

E stays   F# moves up  B stays
(0 steps)  (2 steps)   (0 steps)

Result: Smooth, minimal motion

Example of bad voice leading (large jumps):

Esus2:  E - F# - B
         ↓   ↓    ↓
E major (different octave): E - B - G#

E stays   F# jumps  B jumps
(0 steps)  (6 steps) (8 steps)

Result: Jumpy, disconnected

Golden rule of voice leading: Keep common tones (shared notes) in the same place, and move other notes by the smallest interval possible.

This is why sus → resolution sounds so good — only one note moves, everything else stays put.

Sus2 vs Sus4: When to Use Each

Sus2 (2nd replaces 3rd):

  • Sound: Bright, open, uplifting (the 2nd is close to the root)
  • Resolution: 2nd moves up to the 3rd (F# → G# in E)
  • Use cases: Folk, indie, worship music, ambient pads, open guitar voicings
  • Example artists: Bon Iver, The xx, Sigur Rós

Sus4 (4th replaces 3rd):

  • Sound: Warm, anticipatory, slightly darker (the 4th is higher)
  • Resolution: 4th moves down to the 3rd (A → G# in E)
  • Use cases: Neo-soul, jazz, R&B, classic rock (The Who's "Pinball Wizard"), cinematic scores
  • Example artists: Robert Glasper, Stevie Wonder, The Who

Both can be combined with 7ths for extra sophistication:

Esus2(7):  E - F# - B - D   (adds 7th to sus2)
Esus4(7):  E - A - B - D    (adds 7th to sus4)

Common Mistakes (and How to Avoid Them)

❌ Mistake 1: Including the 3rd in a Sus Chord

Problem: Playing both the 2nd/4th AND the 3rd — this defeats the purpose of the sus chord.

❌ Wrong: E - F# - G# - B   (has both F# and G# — not a sus chord!)
✅ Right: E - F# - B        (Esus2 — only F#, no G#)

Fix: Suspended chords must not have the 3rd. It's either 2nd OR 3rd, never both.

❌ Mistake 2: Jumping Voices Instead of Resolving Smoothly

Problem: Moving all notes when only one needs to move.

❌ Bad voice leading:
Esus2 (E3-F#3-B3) → E major (E4-G#4-B4)   (everything jumps an octave)

✅ Good voice leading:
Esus2 (E3-F#3-B3) → E major (E3-G#3-B3)   (only F# moves to G#)

Fix: Keep common tones stationary. Only move the suspended note (2nd or 4th) to the 3rd.

❌ Mistake 3: Not Letting the Sus Chord Breathe Before Resolving

Problem: Resolving too quickly — the sus chord doesn't have time to create tension.

Fix: Let the sus chord sustain for at least 1-2 bars before resolving. The longer you wait, the more satisfying the resolution feels.

Why This Matters: Real-World Applications

  • 1. Neo-soul and R&B keys: Sus chords are the foundation of modern R&B keyboard playing. Robert Glasper, Cory Henry, and Jacob Collier use sus → resolution constantly for smooth, sophisticated harmony.
  • 2. Indie/worship guitar: Sus2 chords on guitar create that open, ringing indie/worship sound (Hillsong, Bethel Music, Bon Iver). Capo + sus2 voicings = instant modern worship tone.
  • 3. Jazz piano comping: Sus4 → maj7 resolutions are a staple of jazz piano. Creates forward motion without being overly complex.
  • 4. Film scores and ambient music: Suspended chords create atmospheric tension perfect for building emotion in scores (think Hans Zimmer, Max Richter, Ólafur Arnalds).

What It Should Sound Like

When you play Esus2 → E → Asus4 → Amaj7:

  • Esus2 feels suspended: Floating, unresolved, anticipatory (not major or minor)
  • E major feels like relief: The F# → G# motion creates satisfaction and completion
  • Asus4 creates gentle tension: The D note (4th) wants to resolve down to C#
  • Amaj7 feels lush and complete: The resolution + added 7th creates richness
  • Overall vibe: Smooth, jazzy, sophisticated, neo-soul flavored

Reference tracks with sus chords:

  • Robert Glasper - "Afro Blue" — masterclass in sus chord voicings and resolutions
  • Stevie Wonder - "Ribbon in the Sky" — lush sus4 resolutions on keys
  • The Who - "Pinball Wizard" — iconic Asus4 → A guitar riff
  • Bon Iver - "Holocene" — sus2 chords create that open, folky texture
