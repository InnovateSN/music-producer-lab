    Theory

    Jazz Extensions: 9ths, 11ths, 13ths & Alterations

    What You're Actually Making

    You are learning jazz harmony extensions — the art of adding 9ths, 11ths, and 13ths to chords, plus alterations like b9, #9, #11, and b13. These extensions create the lush, sophisticated sound of jazz, neo-soul, R&B, and modern pop.

    While basic chords use only the root, 3rd, 5th, and 7th, jazz harmony stacks additional thirds above the 7th to create richer, more colorful chords. A Cmaj13 chord, for example, contains seven notes: C-E-G-B-D-F#-A.

    The challenge is knowing which extensions to add to which chord types, and how to voice them without creating muddy, unclear harmony.

    The Extension Formula: Stacking Thirds

    Jazz extensions come from continuing the pattern of thirds above the 7th chord:

    Root:  C   (1st)
3rd:   E   (major 3rd above root)
5th:   G   (minor 3rd above 3rd)
7th:   B   (major 3rd above 5th) ← Basic maj7 chord stops here

9th:   D   (minor 3rd above 7th) ← First extension
11th:  F   (minor 3rd above 9th) ← Second extension
13th:  A   (major 3rd above 11th) ← Third extension

    Key Principle: Extensions are named by their interval from the root:

    • 9th = one octave + a 2nd (same pitch class as the 2nd, but higher)
    • 11th = one octave + a 4th (same pitch class as the 4th, but higher)
    • 13th = one octave + a 6th (same pitch class as the 6th, but higher)

    We call them 9th/11th/13th (not 2nd/4th/6th) to distinguish them from the basic chord tones and indicate they're stacked above the 7th.

    Available Extensions by Chord Type

    Not all extensions work on all chord types. Here's what's available for each:

    Major 7th Chords (Imaj7, IVmaj7):

    Cmaj7: C-E-G-B

Available extensions:
- 9th (D): Creates Cmaj9 (C-E-G-B-D) — lush, open
- #11th (F#): Creates Cmaj7(#11) (C-E-G-B-F#) — Lydian, bright
- 13th (A): Creates Cmaj13 (C-E-G-B-D-F#-A) — full, rich

Avoid: Natural 11th (F) — clashes with the major 3rd (E)

    Minor 7th Chords (iim7, iiim7, vim7):

    Dm7: D-F-A-C

Available extensions:
- 9th (E): Creates Dm9 (D-F-A-C-E) — smooth, warm
- 11th (G): Creates Dm11 (D-F-A-C-E-G) — earthy, modal
- 13th (B): Creates Dm13 (all seven notes) — very lush

All extensions work cleanly on minor 7th chords!

    Dominant 7th Chords (V7, secondary dominants):

    G7: G-B-D-F

Available extensions:
- 9th (A): Creates G9 (G-B-D-F-A) — jazz standard
- 13th (E): Creates G13 (G-B-D-F-A-E) — bright, resolving

Avoid: Natural 11th (C) — clashes with the major 3rd (B)

BUT: Dominant chords can use ALTERATIONS (see below)

    Minor 7(b5) / Half-Diminished (viim7b5):

    Bm7(b5): B-D-F-A

Available extensions:
- b9th (C): Creates Bm7(b5,b9) — dark, tense
- 11th (E): Creates Bm11(b5) — complex

Less common to extend these due to inherent tension

    Alterations: b9, #9, #11, b13

    Alterations are chromatic modifications of extensions, used primarily on dominant 7th chords to add tension before resolution.

    The Four Common Alterations:

    1. b9 (Flat Nine):

    G7(b9): G-B-D-F-Ab
Effect: Dark, tense, pulling strongly to resolution
Usage: G7(b9) → Cmaj7 in jazz, creates dissonance that releases

Emotion: Anxious, unresolved, dramatic

    2. #9 (Sharp Nine, "Hendrix Chord"):

    G7(#9): G-B-D-F-A#
Effect: Bluesy, funky, edgy tension
Usage: Jimi Hendrix ("Purple Haze"), funk, blues-rock

Emotion: Aggressive, funky, powerful

    3. #11 (Sharp Eleven):

    G7(#11): G-B-D-F-C#
Effect: Lydian dominant, sophisticated, bright tension
Usage: Jazz standards, modern jazz, film scoring

Emotion: Sophisticated, colorful, floating

    4. b13 (Flat Thirteen):

    G7(b13): G-B-D-F-Eb
Effect: Dark, pulling inward, minor-flavored dominant
Usage: Blues, jazz, resolving to minor chords

Emotion: Dark, brooding, melancholic

    Combining Alterations:

    G7(b9,b13): G-B-D-F-Ab-Eb — very dark, tense
G7(#9,#11): G-B-D-F-A#-C# — very bright, edgy
G7alt ("altered dominant"): includes b9, #9, #11, b13 — maximum tension

    Key Rule: Alterations are only used on dominant 7th chords. Don't use them on major 7th or minor 7th chords — they'll sound wrong.

    Upper Structure Triads

    An upper structure triad is a major or minor triad played above a 7th chord to create complex extensions efficiently.

    How It Works:

    Cmaj7 with upper structure:

Left hand:  C - E (root and 3rd of Cmaj7)
Right hand: D major triad (D-F#-A)

Combined sound: C-E + D-F#-A = Cmaj9(#11,13)

The D major triad creates the 9th, #11th, and 13th all at once!

    Common Upper Structure Triads:

    On Major 7th Chords:

    Cmaj7 + D major = Cmaj9(#11,13)
Cmaj7 + Em = Cmaj9(13)
Cmaj7 + G major = Cmaj9(11) — but avoid natural 11th!

    On Dominant 7th Chords:

    G7 + Db major = G7(b9,#11,b13) — altered dominant
G7 + D major = G9(13)
G7 + Eb major = G7(b9,b13) — dark dominant

    Why Use Upper Structures:

    • Easier to voice on piano — left hand plays root/3rd/7th, right hand plays triad
    • Creates instant complex harmony without calculating each extension
    • Common in jazz piano voicings (Bill Evans, Herbie Hancock)

    Pro Tip: The upper structure triad can be in any inversion. Experiment with different inversions to find the smoothest voice leading.

    Voicing Extended Chords Without Mud

    The biggest challenge with extended chords is avoiding mud — when too many notes in a narrow range create unclear, clashing harmony.

    Rule 1: Omit the 5th

    • The 5th is the least important chord tone (doesn't define major/minor or tension)
    • Drop it to make room for extensions
    • Example: Cmaj9 as C-E-B-D (omit G, the 5th)

    Rule 2: Omit the Root (if bass is playing it)

    • If you have a bass player, they'll cover the root
    • Omit the root from your voicing to free up space
    • Example: Cmaj9 as E-B-D-A (bass plays C)

    Rule 3: Spread the Voicing (Wide Intervals in the Bass)

    • Keep the bass notes (root, 3rd, 7th) in wide intervals (octaves, 10ths)
    • Stack extensions closer together in the upper register
    • Example: C (low) - E (octave up) - B-D-F#-A (clustered high)

    Rule 4: Use the "3rd and 7th" Core

    • The 3rd and 7th are the most important chord tones (define quality and function)
    • Always include them, even if you drop the root and 5th
    • Example: Minimum voicing for Cmaj9: E-B-D (3rd, 7th, 9th)

    Rule 5: Extensions Go High

    • Extensions (9th, 11th, 13th) sound best in the upper register
    • Don't voice a 9th or 13th down in the bass — it'll sound muddy
    • Exception: 9th can sometimes be in mid-range if voiced carefully

    Example Voicings for Cmaj13:

    Full chord: C-E-G-B-D-F#-A (7 notes — too much!)

Practical voicing 1 (piano):
Left hand: C - E (root + 3rd)
Right hand: B - D - A (7th, 9th, 13th)
Omits: G (5th), F# (#11th)

Practical voicing 2 (with bass player):
Omit root, stack extensions:
E - B - D - F# - A (3rd, 7th, 9th, #11th, 13th)

Practical voicing 3 (spread):
C (low) - B (octave up) - E - D - A

    Common Extended Chord Progressions

    1. Jazz ii-V-I with Extensions:

    Dm9 → G13 → Cmaj9
ii9   V13   Imaj9

Effect: Lush, sophisticated, classic jazz sound
Usage: Jazz standards, neo-soul, R&B

    2. Altered Dominant to Major:

    G7alt → Cmaj9
V7alt   Imaj9

(G7alt = G7 with b9, #9, #11, b13 — maximum tension)

Effect: Intense tension → beautiful resolution
Usage: Modern jazz, contemporary classical

    3. Major 7th with #11 (Lydian):

    Cmaj7(#11) → Fmaj7(#11)
I             IV

Effect: Bright, floating, dreamlike
Usage: Film scores, ambient music, Steely Dan

    4. Minor 11th Chain:

    Dm11 → Gm11 → Cm11
iim11  vm11   im11

Effect: Modal, earthy, hypnotic
Usage: Modal jazz (Miles Davis), funk, hip-hop

    5. Dominant 7(#9) (Hendrix Sound):

    E7(#9) → A7(#9)
I7       IV7 (in blues context)

Effect: Funky, aggressive, bluesy
Usage: Blues-rock, funk, R&B

    Genre Applications

    Jazz:

    • Extensions are fundamental — virtually every chord is extended
    • Bill Evans, Herbie Hancock, and McCoy Tyner pioneered modern voicings
    • Standard practice: maj9, min9, dom13, altered dominants

    Neo-Soul & R&B:

    • D'Angelo, Erykah Badu, Robert Glasper use maj9 and min11 chords extensively
    • Creates smooth, sophisticated, emotional harmony
    • Often uses upper structure triads for lush keyboard textures

    Gospel & Church Music:

    • maj9 and min9 chords are standard (warm, uplifting sound)
    • Dominant 13th chords for dramatic resolutions
    • Kirk Franklin, Fred Hammond feature heavy extension use

    Film Scoring:

    • maj7(#11) chords create floating, ethereal atmosphere
    • Altered dominants for tension and drama
    • John Williams, Thomas Newman use extended harmony extensively

    Pop (Advanced):

    • Steely Dan built their entire sound on jazz extensions
    • Radiohead uses maj9 and min11 chords for atmospheric songs
    • Modern pop producers add 9ths for sophistication (The Weeknd, SZA)

    Common Mistakes to Avoid

    • Using natural 11th on major chords: The 11th (4th scale degree) clashes harshly with the major 3rd. Always use #11 on major 7th chords, or omit the 11th entirely.
    • Voicing extensions too low: Extensions sound best in the upper register. A 9th or 13th voiced in the bass creates mud and loses its color.
    • Playing all seven notes: A Cmaj13 doesn't need all seven notes. Omit the 5th (and sometimes root) to keep the voicing clear.
    • Using alterations on non-dominant chords: b9, #9, #11, b13 are only for dominant 7th chords. Using them on major or minor 7th chords sounds wrong.
    • Over-extending in simple contexts: If you're writing a simple pop song, a maj13 chord might be overkill. Match the complexity to the genre and context.
    • Ignoring voice leading: Extensions must still follow good voice leading. Don't jump extensions around randomly — move them smoothly like any other chord tone.

    Why This Matters

    Jazz harmony extensions are the final piece of your advanced harmony toolkit because they:

    • Create lush, sophisticated sounds — essential for jazz, neo-soul, R&B, and modern pop
    • Add harmonic color — a maj9 sounds warmer and richer than a plain major triad
    • Enable professional voicings — omitting the 5th and using upper structures sounds polished and clear
    • Unlock entire genres — you cannot play jazz, neo-soul, or gospel authentically without extensions
    • Give you infinite harmonic options — with 9ths, 11ths, 13ths, and alterations, you can create thousands of unique chord sounds

    Combined with everything you've learned (secondary dominants, tritone substitution, chromatic reharmonization), you now have complete mastery of advanced harmony. You can create progressions that are sophisticated, surprising, emotional, and perfectly voiced across any genre.
