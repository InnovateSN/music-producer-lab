Metering & Analysis: See What You Hear

Professional mastering relies on accurate metering as much as trained ears. LUFS meters measure perceived loudness, peak meters catch digital overs, spectrum analyzers reveal frequency balance, and phase correlation meters ensure mono compatibility. Understanding these tools lets you make objective decisions and meet streaming platform requirements. This lesson covers every essential meter you need for professional mastering in your DAW.

LUFS (Loudness Units)

LUFS measures perceived loudness, accounting for how humans hear. Streaming targets: Spotify -14 LUFS, Apple Music -16 LUFS, YouTube -14 LUFS. Integrated LUFS = average loudness; Short-term = last 3 seconds; Momentary = 400ms window.

True Peak Metering

True Peak catches inter-sample peaks that standard meters miss. Keep true peak below -1 dBTP (or -2 dBTP for safety) to prevent clipping during codec conversion. Regular peak meters can miss peaks that cause distortion on streaming platforms.

Spectrum Analysis

Spectrum analyzers show frequency distribution. Compare your master to references—look for similar overall shape. Watch for excessive sub (<30 Hz), muddy low-mids (200-400 Hz), or harsh highs (3-5 kHz). Use EQ to address imbalances you see.

Phase Correlation

The correlation meter shows stereo relationship: +1 = mono, 0 = uncorrelated, -1 = out of phase. Keep it mostly positive (above 0). Negative correlation = phase cancellation on mono systems. Wide stereo is fine, but watch for excessive negative readings.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mixing with Your Eyes: Meters inform decisions, but don't let them override your ears. A track can look "wrong" on a meter but sound perfect. Use meters to confirm what you hear, not replace listening.
Ignoring True Peak: Standard peak meters miss inter-sample peaks. Your master might show -0.3 dB peak but actually clip after encoding. Always use true peak metering and leave headroom.
Chasing Loudness Numbers: Don't crush your master to hit a LUFS target. Streaming platforms normalize anyway—a dynamic -14 LUFS master will sound better than a crushed -8 LUFS master after normalization.

Why This Matters

  • Platform Compliance: Meet streaming platform requirements to avoid unwanted normalization or rejection.
  • Objective Reference: Meters give you data when ear fatigue sets in or your room acoustics deceive you.
  • Quality Control: Catch phase problems, clipping, and frequency imbalances before release.
  • Professional Standards: Broadcast and streaming standards require specific loudness targets—meters ensure compliance.
