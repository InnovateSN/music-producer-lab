Chord Construction: Building Harmony from Intervals

Every chord in music is built from a simple recipe: stacked intervals of thirds. A triad uses three notes (root, 3rd, 5th), and a seventh chord adds one more note on top. The type of third you stack -- major (4 semitones) or minor (3 semitones) -- determines whether a chord sounds happy, sad, tense, or unstable. When you work in a DAW with MIDI, understanding chord construction means you can build any chord from scratch on a piano roll, name any chord you see, and make deliberate harmonic choices instead of guessing. This lesson covers triads, seventh chords, inversions, and voicing -- the complete toolkit for constructing harmony.

Triad Construction

Root + 3rd + 5th

A triad is three notes built by stacking two thirds above a root note. The quality of those thirds determines the chord type: Major = major 3rd + minor 3rd. Minor = minor 3rd + major 3rd. Diminished = minor 3rd + minor 3rd. Augmented = major 3rd + major 3rd.

Why it matters: Triads are the building blocks of all harmony. Every pop, rock, and electronic track is built on triads.

Seventh Chords

Triads + One More Third

Stack another third on top of a triad and you get a seventh chord. Major 7th (Maj7) = major triad + major 7th. Dominant 7th (7) = major triad + minor 7th. Minor 7th (m7) = minor triad + minor 7th. Half-diminished (m7b5) = diminished triad + minor 7th. Diminished 7th (dim7) = diminished triad + diminished 7th.

Why it matters: Seventh chords add color and sophistication. They are essential in jazz, R&B, neo-soul, lo-fi, and any genre that goes beyond basic pop harmony.

Chord Inversions

Same Notes, Different Order

An inversion changes which note is on the bottom. Root position: root is the lowest note (C-E-G). 1st inversion: 3rd is the lowest (E-G-C). 2nd inversion: 5th is the lowest (G-C-E). The chord name stays the same, but the sound and feel change.

Why it matters: Inversions create smooth voice leading between chords. Instead of jumping around the keyboard, chords connect smoothly -- essential for professional-sounding progressions.

Voicing & Spacing

How You Spread the Notes

Voicing is about how you arrange chord tones across the register. Close voicing: all notes within one octave (tight, compact). Open voicing: notes spread across two or more octaves (wide, spacious). You can also double notes, omit the 5th, or rearrange the order of upper notes.

Why it matters: Two producers can play the same chord but get completely different textures by changing the voicing. In a MIDI piano roll, voicing determines whether a chord sounds muddy or clear.

Common Mistakes in Chord Construction

Stacking the Wrong Interval Size

A major third is 4 semitones and a minor third is 3 semitones. Mixing these up gives you the wrong chord quality entirely -- for example, stacking two major thirds gives you an augmented chord, not a major one.

Fix: Count semitones on a keyboard. Major = 4, minor = 3. For a major triad: 4 semitones + 3 semitones. For a minor triad: 3 + 4.

Confusing Major and Minor Chord Symbols

"Cm" means C minor, but "C" alone means C major. "Cmaj7" means a major triad with a major 7th, while "C7" (no "maj") means a major triad with a minor 7th (dominant). These subtle naming differences cause frequent confusion.

Fix: Remember the rule -- a plain letter (C, D, G) always means major. Lowercase "m" after the letter means minor. "maj7" = major 7th interval, plain "7" = dominant (minor 7th interval).

Ignoring Inversions and Voice Leading

Playing every chord in root position creates large jumps between chords. In a MIDI piano roll, this looks like notes jumping up and down instead of moving smoothly. The result sounds choppy and disconnected.

Fix: When moving between chords, find the inversion that keeps common tones in place and moves other notes by the smallest distance possible. This is called voice leading.

Voicing Chords Too Low on the Keyboard

Stacking thirds in the low register (below C3) creates a muddy, indistinct sound. The frequency bands overlap and the individual notes lose clarity, especially with seventh chords.

Fix: Keep chord voicings in the C3-C5 range for clarity. Use open voicings or place only the root in the bass register with the rest of the chord higher up.

Why Chord Construction Changes Everything

  • Build Any Chord from Scratch: Instead of memorizing shapes, you can construct any chord on any root note using interval formulas. Major, minor, diminished, augmented, seventh -- all from one system.
  • Name Any Chord You See: When you encounter notes stacked in a MIDI piano roll, you can identify the chord type by analyzing the intervals between the notes.
  • Control Emotional Impact: Major chords sound bright; minor chords sound dark; diminished chords create tension; augmented chords sound dreamlike. Choosing chord quality deliberately lets you shape the emotion of your music.
  • Smooth Chord Progressions: Understanding inversions and voice leading means your chords flow naturally into each other instead of jumping around -- the difference between amateur and professional harmony.
  • Foundation for Advanced Harmony: Extensions (9ths, 11ths, 13ths), altered chords, and jazz voicings all build on the triad and seventh chord system covered in this lesson.
