Genre-Specific Mastering: Different Approaches for Different Styles

Different genres have different mastering conventions. An EDM master prioritizes loudness and punch, while a jazz recording needs dynamics and natural sound. Hip-hop demands heavy sub bass; rock wants energy and aggression. Understanding these conventions helps you deliver masters that meet genre expectations while maintaining quality. This lesson covers mastering approaches for major genres.

EDM & Electronic

Loud and punchy. Target -8 to -10 LUFS for club play. Tight limiting, aggressive loudness. Emphasize transients and sub bass. Wide stereo image in highs. Check on club systems if possible. Drops should hit hard.

Hip-Hop & Trap

Heavy sub bass, clear vocals. 808s need to translate on all systems—add harmonics for small speakers. Vocals should cut through. Target -9 to -12 LUFS. Punchy drums, present hi-hats. Less width in the low end; keep bass mono.

Rock & Metal

Energy and aggression. Preserve dynamics for impact—don't over-limit. Target -10 to -14 LUFS depending on subgenre. Guitars need presence without harshness. Drums should punch through dense guitars. Analog-style saturation adds warmth.

Pop & Acoustic

Polished and radio-ready. Pop: -9 to -11 LUFS, bright, wide, clean. Acoustic/jazz: -14 to -18 LUFS, preserve dynamics and natural sound. Vocals are king in both—clarity without harshness. Subtle processing; don't crush life out of the performance.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

One-Size-Fits-All Processing: EDM limiting settings will destroy a jazz recording. Adjust your approach for each genre—listen to commercial references and match their character.
Ignoring Genre Expectations: A hip-hop track with no sub bass won't satisfy the audience. A metal track without aggression feels weak. Know what the genre demands and deliver it.
Over-Processing Acoustic Music: Classical, jazz, and folk need minimal processing. The performance IS the product. Heavy limiting and EQ can destroy the natural beauty that makes these genres special.

Why This Matters

  • Audience Expectations: Each genre's audience has specific expectations. Meet them.
  • Competitive Sound: Your master competes with commercial releases in that genre.
  • Client Satisfaction: Artists want their music to fit within their genre's sonic landscape.
  • Versatility: Understanding multiple genres makes you a more valuable mastering engineer.
