    Theory

    Chromatic Reharmonization: Advanced Voice Leading

    What You're Actually Making

    You are learning chromatic reharmonization — advanced techniques for inserting chromatic chords between diatonic chords to create smooth voice leading, dramatic harmonic shifts, and emotionally powerful progressions. These techniques go beyond secondary dominants and tritone substitution to include chromatic passing chords, chromatic mediants, and augmented sixth chords.

    Chromatic reharmonization is the secret behind film scores, dramatic pop ballads, and sophisticated jazz arrangements. It creates harmonic movement that feels both surprising and inevitable.

    Technique 1: Chromatic Passing Chords

    A chromatic passing chord is a chord inserted between two diatonic chords, where the bass moves by chromatic half-steps. This creates smooth, stepwise bass motion and harmonic interest.

    Formula:

    1. Identify two diatonic chords that are a whole-step apart
    2. Insert a chord with a root on the chromatic note between them
    3. The passing chord is often a dominant 7th or diminished 7th

    Classic Example: I → #I°7 → ii

    C major: C → C#°7 → Dm
         I   #I°7   ii

Bass: C → C# → D (chromatic ascent)

    The C#°7 is a chromatic passing chord between C and Dm. It has no diatonic function in C major — it exists purely to create smooth chromatic motion.

    More Chromatic Passing Chord Examples:

    ii → #ii°7 → iii
Dm → D#°7 → Em (bass: D → D# → E)

IV → #IV°7 → V
F → F#°7 → G (bass: F → F# → G)

I → bVII7 → vi
C → Bb7 → Am (bass: C → Bb → A, chromatic descent)

vi → bvi7 → V
Am → Abm7 → G7 (bass: A → Ab → G, chromatic descent)

    Why Diminished 7th Chords Work Best: Diminished 7th chords (like C#°7) are symmetrical — every note is a minor 3rd apart. This creates equal tension in all directions, allowing smooth voice leading to any nearby chord. They're the ultimate chromatic passing chords.

    Technique 2: Chromatic Mediants

    A chromatic mediant is a chord whose root is a major or minor third away from the current chord, but with a different quality (major vs minor). These chords share one or two common tones but create dramatic harmonic shifts.

    The Six Chromatic Mediants from C Major:

    Upper chromatic mediants (a third above):
- C → E   (I → III)   - share G, change from C to E
- C → Eb  (I → bIII)  - share G, change from C to Eb

Lower chromatic mediants (a third below):
- C → Ab  (I → bVI)   - share C and Eb
- C → A   (I → VI)    - share C and E

Also:
- C → Eb  (I → bIII)  - Neapolitan feeling
- C → E   (I → III)   - bright, unexpected

    Classic Chromatic Mediant Progressions:

    1. I → bVI → bVII → I (Film Scoring Favorite):

    C → Ab → Bb → C
Effect: Dramatic, cinematic, mysterious
Usage: Hans Zimmer, John Williams, video game scores

    2. I → bIII → IV (Romantic, Lush):

    C → Eb → F
Effect: Warm, nostalgic, emotional depth
Usage: Ballads, film romance themes

    3. I → VI → IV (Deceptive Brightness):

    C → A → F
Effect: Unexpected shift to brightness, then resolution
Usage: Pop choruses, musical theater

    Why Chromatic Mediants Work: They share common tones (partial connection) but have roots that are chromatic (surprising shift). The ear hears both familiarity and surprise simultaneously, creating emotional impact.

    Technique 3: Augmented Sixth Chords

    An augmented sixth chord is a chromatic pre-dominant chord that strongly resolves to the dominant (V). It's called "augmented sixth" because it contains an interval of an augmented 6th above the bass note.

    The Three Types:

    1. Italian Augmented Sixth (It+6):

    In C major: Ab - C - F#
Bass: Ab (moves to G, the dominant)

Intervals: Root, major 3rd, augmented 6th
Resolution: It+6 → V (Ab-C-F# → G-B-D-G)

Voice leading:
- Ab → G (down half-step to V root)
- C → B (down half-step to V 3rd)
- F# → G (up half-step to V root)

    2. German Augmented Sixth (Ger+6):

    In C major: Ab - C - Eb - F#
Bass: Ab

Intervals: Root, major 3rd, perfect 5th, augmented 6th
Resolution: Ger+6 → V (or I6/4 → V to avoid parallel 5ths)

More common than Italian, fuller sound

    3. French Augmented Sixth (Fr+6):

    In C major: Ab - C - D - F#
Bass: Ab

Intervals: Root, major 3rd, augmented 4th, augmented 6th
Resolution: Fr+6 → V

Brighter sound due to the D (major 2nd scale degree)

    How to Use Augmented Sixth Chords:

    Common progression: iv → Ger+6 → V → I

C major: Fm → Ab-C-Eb-F# → G7 → C
         iv   Ger+6         V    I

Effect: Dark (minor iv) → intense chromatic tension → resolution
Usage: Classical music, dramatic film moments, sophisticated pop

    Why They're Powerful: The augmented sixth interval (Ab to F# = augmented 6th, enharmonic to minor 7th) creates strong outward tension that resolves to the dominant. This is one of the strongest pre-dominant resolutions in Western harmony.

    Combining Chromatic Techniques

    The most sophisticated progressions combine multiple chromatic reharmonization techniques:

    Example 1: Chromatic Passing + Chromatic Mediant:

    C major: C → C#°7 → Dm → Ab → G7 → C
         I   passing ii   bVI  V    I

Analysis:
- C → C#°7 → Dm: chromatic passing chord
- Dm → Ab: chromatic mediant (iv → bVI relationship)
- Ab → G7: dramatic descent to dominant

    Example 2: Secondary Dominant + Tritone Sub + Chromatic Mediant:

    C major: Em → Bb7 → Eb → Ab7 → Dm → Db7 → C
         iii  V/ii  bIII bVI   ii   subV  I

Analysis:
- Bb7 (tritone sub of E7, which is V/ii)
- Eb (chromatic mediant)
- Ab7 (V/ii, secondary dominant to Dm)
- Db7 (tritone sub of G7)

    This progression uses every advanced technique you've learned in lessons 21-23. It's extremely sophisticated and creates continuous harmonic motion.

    Genre Applications

    Film Scoring:

    • Chromatic mediants (I → bVI, I → bIII) are foundational to modern film scoring
    • Hans Zimmer, John Williams, and Howard Shore use these extensively
    • Example: "The Dark Knight" theme uses C → Ab → Bb chromatic mediant motion
    • Augmented sixth chords create dramatic tension before climactic moments

    Classical Music:

    • Beethoven, Chopin, and Wagner pioneered chromatic reharmonization
    • Augmented sixth chords are standard in classical cadences
    • Chromatic passing chords appear in almost every Romantic-era piece

    Jazz:

    • Chromatic passing diminished 7th chords are everywhere in bebop and swing
    • Example: "I'll Remember April" uses multiple chromatic passing chords
    • Modal jazz (Miles Davis, Herbie Hancock) uses chromatic mediants

    Pop & Rock:

    • Radiohead frequently uses chromatic mediants (I → bIII, I → bVI)
    • The Beatles used chromatic passing chords ("Strawberry Fields Forever")
    • Ballads use chromatic mediants for emotional lift in choruses

    Video Game Music:

    • Nobuo Uematsu (Final Fantasy) uses chromatic mediants for dramatic shifts
    • Koji Kondo (Zelda) uses chromatic passing chords for exploration themes

    Voice Leading Principles for Chromatic Harmony

    Chromatic reharmonization requires careful voice leading to sound smooth rather than jarring:

    Rule 1: Move by Half-Steps When Possible

    • Chromatic notes should resolve by half-step (up or down)
    • Example: In C#°7 → Dm, the C# resolves to D (up half-step)

    Rule 2: Maintain Common Tones

    • Chromatic mediants share 1-2 common tones — keep them in the same voice
    • Example: C major (C-E-G) → Ab major (Ab-C-Eb) — keep C in the same voice

    Rule 3: Resolve Tendency Tones

    • Leading tones (especially in augmented sixth chords) must resolve up
    • 7ths must resolve down
    • Don't leave chromaticism unresolved — complete the motion

    Rule 4: Use Stepwise Bass Motion

    • Chromatic passing chords work best when the bass moves by half or whole steps
    • Avoid large leaps in the bass during chromatic sections

    Common Mistakes to Avoid

    • Overusing chromatic harmony: Too much chromaticism makes the key ambiguous. Use chromatic chords strategically, then return to diatonic harmony to re-establish the key.
    • Poor voice leading: Chromatic notes must resolve properly. If you use F# in a Ger+6, it must move to G (the dominant root). Don't leave chromatic notes hanging.
    • Ignoring the melody: Chromatic chords can clash with melodies. Check that melody notes are chord tones or appropriate tensions, not avoid notes.
    • Forgetting the function: Augmented sixth chords are pre-dominant — they must resolve to V. Using Ger+6 → I doesn't work; the tension goes unresolved.
    • Random chromatic mediants: Not all chromatic mediants work equally well. I → bVI and I → bIII are proven; I → bII is rare and awkward. Use established patterns first.

    Why This Matters

    Chromatic reharmonization is the final frontier of advanced harmony because it:

    • Creates dramatic emotional shifts — chromatic mediants can instantly change the mood
    • Enables ultra-smooth voice leading — chromatic passing chords connect diatonic chords seamlessly
    • Adds harmonic sophistication — augmented sixth chords sound complex and professional
    • Unlocks film scoring techniques — essential for cinematic, dramatic music
    • Bridges classical and modern — these techniques work in Beethoven and Hans Zimmer alike

    Combined with secondary dominants (Lesson 21) and tritone substitution (Lesson 22), chromatic reharmonization gives you complete control over harmonic movement. You can now create progressions that are smooth, surprising, dramatic, and emotionally powerful across any genre.
