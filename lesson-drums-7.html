What Are Ghost Notes?

Ghost notes are very soft drum hits played between the main beats. On a real drum kit, they're the quiet "chatter" that drummers add to fill space and create groove. In programming, we achieve this with low velocity notes.

üéØ Why Ghost Notes Matter: They add "shuffle" and "pocket" to your drums without changing the core pattern. This is what separates amateur beats from professional productions.

Understanding Velocity

Velocity (0-127) controls how hard a note is played. In drum programming:

üëª Ghost (15-40)

Barely audible texture. Adds shuffle without competing with main hits.

üîâ Soft (40-70)

Quiet supporting notes. Secondary hi-hats, light percussion.

üîä Normal (70-100)

Standard playing volume. Main groove notes.

üí• Accent (100-127)

Emphasized hits. Downbeats, crash cymbals, fill peaks.

Where to Place Ghost Notes

Ghost notes typically go on the off-beats (the "e" and "a" of 16th notes):

ü•Å Classic Ghost Note Pattern (Snare)

Beat:    1  e  &  a  2  e  &  a  3  e  &  a  4  e  &  a
Main:    -  -  -  -  X  -  -  -  -  -  -  -  X  -  -  -
Ghost:   -  x  -  x  -  x  -  x  -  x  -  x  -  x  -  x

X = Accent (100+ velocity)
x = Ghost (25-35 velocity)

Common Ghost Note Placements

  • Before the backbeat: Ghost on the "a" of 1 and 3 (just before beats 2 and 4)
  • After the backbeat: Ghost on the "e" of 2 and 4 (just after the snare)
  • Continuous 16ths: Ghost on every 16th note except the main hits
  • Swing placement: Ghost on the "&" counts for swing feel

Key Takeaways

  • Ghost notes are soft hits (15-40 velocity) that add texture and groove
  • Place them on off-beats (the "e" and "a" of 16th notes)
  • Use dynamic contrast between accents and ghosts for professional feel
  • Ghost notes make patterns feel human and alive
  • Less is more‚Äîghost notes should be felt, not heard
