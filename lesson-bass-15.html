    Reggae & Dub Bass: Heavy Low End

    What You're Actually Making

    You're programming reggae bass — the deep, heavy low-end foundation that defines reggae, dub, and influenced electronic bass music from dubstep to UK garage. Reggae bass is fundamentally different from rock, funk, and R&B: it emphasizes beats 1 and 3 (opposite of rock's 2 & 4), uses massive sub-bass weight, and creates hypnotic, meditative grooves.

    Reggae bass legends like Robbie Shakespeare (Sly & Robbie), Aston "Family Man" Barrett (Bob Marley & The Wailers), and Lloyd Parks created the template: deep, round, minimal bass with space for dub effects. Dub bass, pioneered by producers like King Tubby and Lee "Scratch" Perry, takes this further — adding delay, reverb, and filter sweeps to create atmospheric soundscapes.

    This lesson teaches the rhythmic DNA of reggae and the sonic foundation of modern bass music culture.

    The One Drop: Reggae's Signature Rhythm

    Reggae rhythm is built on the "one drop" — a drum pattern that creates the signature laid-back groove:

    Standard Rock/Pop (backbeat):
Kick:  |1---3---|1---3---| (1 & 3)
Snare: |--2---4-|--2---4-| (2 & 4)

Reggae "One Drop":
Kick:  |----3---|----3---| (ONLY on 3)
Snare: |--2---4-|--2---4-| (2 & 4, same as rock)

The kick DROPS on beat 3, creating that hypnotic pulse

    Why This Works:

    • Beat 3 emphasis: Creates laid-back, behind-the-beat feel
    • Open beat 1: Gives space, makes the groove breathe
    • Hypnotic: Repetitive, meditative, trance-inducing
    • Bass + kick lock: Bass plays with kick on beat 3

    Bass in One Drop:

    Bar 1:
Beat 1: (root note or rest)
Beat 2: (approach or rest)
Beat 3: ROOT NOTE (locks with kick) — HEAVY
Beat 4: (fifth or rest)

The bass LOCKS with the kick on beat 3
Creates massive low-end impact

    Variation: "Rockers" Style:

    Kick: |1-2-3-4-|1-2-3-4-| (Four-on-the-floor)
Bass: Still emphasizes 1 & 3, but with steady kick

More driving, influenced reggae-rock and dub techno

    Reggae Bass Tone: Deep, Round, Minimal

    Reggae bass has a distinctive sonic character — deep fundamental with minimal mid-range:

    Frequency Characteristics:

    Sub-Bass (40-80 Hz):
- HEAVY presence, felt in the chest
- Pure, round sine wave character
- No excessive harmonics

Low-Mids (80-200 Hz):
- Warm body, not aggressive
- Rolled off compared to funk or rock
- No "clank" or "grind"

Mids (200-1000 Hz):
- Minimal, almost absent
- Creates space for rhythm guitar/keys
- "Dub" sound = less mids

Highs (1k+ Hz):
- No treble, no brightness
- Focus is 100% on low end

    Classic Reggae Bass Sound:

    • Fender Precision Bass: Flatwound strings, tone knob rolled off
    • Played with fingers: Soft attack, no pick brightness
    • DI + slight compression: Natural, unprocessed tone
    • Low tuning: Sometimes tuned down (D-G-C-F) for extra weight

    Programming Reggae Bass (MIDI):

    • Waveform: Sine or triangle (not sawtooth/square)
    • Filter: Low-pass at 200-400 Hz (remove highs)
    • Envelope: Slow attack (20-40ms), long sustain
    • No brightness: Pure low-end focus

    Dub Bass: Space, Delay, and Atmosphere

    Dub is the instrumental, effects-heavy remix style born in Jamaica in the 1960s-70s. Dub bass uses space and effects to create psychedelic soundscapes:

    Key Dub Techniques:

    1. Space and Silence:

    Verse: Full bass line
Chorus: DROP OUT bass (just drums)
Bridge: Bass with heavy delay/reverb
Drop: Bass SLAMS back in with impact

Silence is used as a compositional tool

    2. Tape Delay (Echo):

    Original note: E (beat 1)
Delay 1: E (beat 2, -6 dB)
Delay 2: E (beat 3, -12 dB)
Delay 3: E (beat 4, -18 dB)

Classic: 1/4 note delay, 30-50% feedback
Creates rhythmic echo pattern

    3. Filter Sweeps:

    Low-pass filter opens/closes over time
Automated: Closed (dark) → Open (bright) → Closed

Creates movement, breathing effect
Classic dub production technique

    4. Reverb (Spring/Plate):

    • Short decay (0.8-1.5s): Adds depth without mud
    • Pre-delay (20-40ms): Separates reverb from dry signal
    • High-pass reverb: Keep sub clean, reverb on mids/highs

    5. Distortion/Saturation (Subtle):

    • Tape saturation: Warm, rounded harmonics
    • Tube overdrive: Adds grit without harshness
    • Used sparingly: Reggae bass is about purity, not aggression

    Classic Reggae Bass Patterns

    1. Walking Bass (Roots Reggae):

    C → D → E♭ → E (ascending chromatic walk)
E → D → C → B (descending walk)

Simple, stepwise motion
Fills space between chord changes

    2. Bubble Pattern (Digital Reggae/Dancehall):

    Root on 1, octave jump on 2, root on 3, fifth on 4
E - E' - E - B

Bouncy, energetic, modern dancehall sound
Popularized in 1980s digital reggae

    3. One-Note Meditation:

    Just the root, on beat 3, every bar
E______3_______E______3_______

Hypnotic, minimal, allows dub effects to shine
Classic King Tubby/Scientist production

    4. Steppers (Four-on-the-Floor):

    Root on every beat: E - E - E - E

Driving, modern roots reggae
Influenced by disco and electronic music

    Reggae & Dub's Influence on Electronic Music

    Reggae bass is the DNA of modern electronic bass music. Here's the lineage:

    1. Sound System Culture → Jungle/Drum & Bass:

    • Jamaican sound systems: Massive sub-bass rigs
    • UK jungle (1990s): Reggae bass + breakbeats
    • Reese bass: Detuned sawtooth = reggae sub weight + aggression

    2. Dub → UK Garage:

    • Dub delays and space: Carried into garage production
    • 2-step rhythm: Reggae one drop influence
    • Sub-bass focus: 40-80 Hz emphasis from reggae

    3. Dub → Dubstep:

    • Name says it all: "Dub" + "step"
    • Half-time rhythm: Reggae/dub tempo feel (70 BPM sound at 140 BPM)
    • Wobble bass: Dub filter sweeps + synthesis
    • Space and silence: Dub compositional techniques

    4. Dub Techno:

    • Basic Channel (Germany): Techno + dub aesthetics
    • Heavy reverb/delay: Classic dub effects on 4/4 techno
    • Minimal, hypnotic: Reggae's meditative quality

    Modern Producers Using Reggae/Dub:

    • Skream, Benga: Early dubstep pioneers
    • Burial: Dub atmosphere + UK garage
    • Bonobo, Floating Points: Dub delays in downtempo
    • Bicep, Peggy Gou: Dub techno influences

    Common Mistakes

    • Emphasizing Beats 2 & 4: That's rock/pop. Reggae emphasizes beats 1 & 3. If your bass sounds like rock, you're on the wrong beats.
    • Too Much Mid-Range: Reggae bass is 90% sub-bass (40-120 Hz). If it sounds "clanky" or "grindy," roll off mids above 200 Hz.
    • Rushing the Beat: Reggae is LAID BACK. Play slightly behind the beat, never on top or ahead. Rushing kills the groove.
    • Over-Processing Dub Effects: Dub delays should be musical and rhythmic, not chaotic. Use 1/4 or 1/8 note delays locked to tempo.
    • Playing Too Many Notes: Reggae is minimal and meditative. If you're playing more than 4-6 notes per bar, you're overplaying.

    Why This Matters

    Reggae and dub bass created modern bass music culture. From dubstep to UK garage, drum & bass to dub techno, reggae's sub-bass emphasis and dub's spatial effects are everywhere. Learning reggae bass gives you:

    • Sub-bass mastery: How to create weight and impact at 40-80 Hz
    • Rhythmic inversion: Understanding beats 1 & 3 vs 2 & 4
    • Dub production: Delay, reverb, space as compositional tools
    • Sound system culture: Bass designed for physical impact
    • Genre lineage: Understanding jungle, dubstep, garage, dub techno origins

    Producers like Skream, Burial, Mala (Digital Mystikz), and Loefah built dubstep on reggae/dub foundations. Even house and techno producers use dub delays and sub-bass techniques. Understanding reggae makes you fluent in the language of bass.

    What's Next

    Now that you've mastered reggae and dub bass with its heavy low-end weight and spatial effects, you're ready for Lesson 16: UK Garage & Grime Bass. While reggae is laid-back and hypnotic, UK garage is skippy and energetic with 2-step rhythms, and grime is dark and aggressive with sub-bass focus.
