    Theory

    Secondary Dominants: Tonicization Techniques

    What You're Actually Making

    You are learning secondary dominants — dominant 7th chords that temporarily tonicize (treat as a tonic) diatonic chords other than the actual tonic. This technique creates tension, momentum, and harmonic interest by borrowing the V→I resolution feeling and applying it to other chords in the key.

    In C major, instead of just using the diatonic V7 (G7 → C), you can use:

    • V/ii (A7 → Dm): Makes Dm feel like a temporary tonic
    • V/iii (B7 → Em): Tonicizes Em
    • V/IV (C7 → F): The tonic chord becomes dominant!
    • V/V (D7 → G7): Sets up an extended approach to the real dominant
    • V/vi (E7 → Am): Creates jazzy, deceptive movement

    Each secondary dominant is a major triad with a minor 7th, built on the 5th scale degree of the chord you want to tonicize.

    The Formula: Building Secondary Dominants

    To create a secondary dominant for any diatonic chord:

    1. Identify the target chord (the chord you want to tonicize)
    2. Find its 5th (count up a perfect 5th from the target's root)
    3. Build a major triad on that 5th
    4. Add a minor 7th to create a dominant 7th chord

    Example: Creating V/ii in C Major

    Target chord: ii = Dm (D-F-A)
Its 5th: A (count D → E → F → G → A)
Major triad on A: A major (A-C#-E)
Add minor 7th: A7 (A-C#-E-G)
Resolution: A7 → Dm

    Complete Secondary Dominants in C Major:

    V/ii:  A7  (A-C#-E-G)  → Dm
V/iii: B7  (B-D#-F#-A) → Em
V/IV:  C7  (C-E-G-Bb)  → F
V/V:   D7  (D-F#-A-C)  → G7
V/vi:  E7  (E-G#-B-D)  → Am

    Key observation: Notice that secondary dominants introduce chromatic notes (notes outside the key). In C major, the diatonic notes are C-D-E-F-G-A-B. Secondary dominants add C#, D#, F#, G#, and Bb — these accidentals create harmonic tension that resolves when moving to the target chord.

    Voice Leading in Secondary Dominants

    Secondary dominants are powerful because they create the same leading tone → tonic and 7th → 3rd voice leading as a regular V7→I resolution.

    The Two Essential Voice Leading Rules:

    1. Raised leading tone resolves up by half-step to the root of the target chord
    2. The 7th resolves down by step to the 3rd of the target chord

    Example: A7 → Dm Voice Leading

    A7 chord tones:    A  - C# - E  - G
                   ↓    ↓    ↓    ↓
Dm chord tones:    A  - D  - F  - F

Voice leading:
- C# (3rd of A7, leading tone to D) → D (root of Dm) [up half-step]
- G (7th of A7) → F (3rd of Dm) [down whole-step]
- A and E can stay or move smoothly

    Why This Matters: This voice leading creates a strong, convincing resolution even though Dm isn't the actual tonic of the key. The ear temporarily "believes" Dm is home.

    Common Secondary Dominant Progressions

    Here are the most frequently used secondary dominant progressions across multiple genres:

    1. V/IV → IV → V → I (Extended Plagal)

    C major: C7 → F → G7 → C
Usage: Beatles, classic rock, gospel
Effect: Smooth, warm approach to IV, then traditional cadence

    2. V/V → V → I (Double Dominant)

    C major: D7 → G7 → C
Usage: Classical, jazz standards, musical theater
Effect: Extended dominant approach, builds anticipation

    3. Circle of Fifths with Secondary Dominants

    C major: (E7 → Am) → (A7 → Dm) → (D7 → G7) → C
          V/vi  vi     V/ii  ii     V/V   V     I
Usage: Jazz, bebop, sophisticated pop (Steely Dan)
Effect: Strong, continuous forward motion

    4. V/vi → vi → IV → V → I (Deceptive to Minor)

    C major: E7 → Am → F → G7 → C
Usage: Ballads, melancholic pop, film scores
Effect: Unexpected shift to minor, emotional depth

    5. V/iii → iii → vi → ii → V → I

    C major: B7 → Em → Am → Dm → G7 → C
Usage: Jazz, bossa nova, R&B
Effect: Chromatic descent, sophisticated sound

    Genre-Specific Applications

    Jazz Standards:

    • Secondary dominants are foundational to jazz harmony
    • Common in ii-V-I progressions: use V/ii to approach the ii chord
    • Example: "Autumn Leaves" uses V/ii, V/vi, V/V extensively

    Blues & Rock:

    • V/IV → IV is the classic "blues turnaround" in bar 9-10 of 12-bar blues
    • Example: In blues in E, use B7 → E7 (V/IV → IV) before returning to E
    • Creates movement without leaving the blues feel

    Classical & Film Scoring:

    • Mozart and Beethoven frequently used V/V → V → I for cadences
    • Film composers use chains of secondary dominants for building tension
    • Example: Hans Zimmer uses V/vi → vi for emotional depth

    Pop & R&B:

    • Modern pop often uses V/IV → IV to refresh repetitive progressions
    • Example: Instead of I-IV-V-I, use I-V/IV-IV-V-I for more interest
    • R&B uses V/ii and V/vi for smooth, jazzy chord movement

    Advanced Technique: Deceptive Resolutions

    Secondary dominants don't always have to resolve to their expected target. You can use deceptive resolutions for surprise and complexity:

    Example 1: V/ii → bVI (instead of ii)

    C major: A7 → Ab (instead of A7 → Dm)
Effect: Unexpected, cinematic, mysterious

    Example 2: V/V → vi (instead of V)

    C major: D7 → Am (instead of D7 → G7)
Effect: Deceptive cadence with chromatic approach

    Pro Tip: Deceptive resolutions work best when you've already established the expected pattern. Use them after the listener has heard a few traditional resolutions.

    Common Mistakes to Avoid

    • Forgetting the accidentals: Secondary dominants must be major triads with minor 7ths. If you use A minor instead of A7, it's not a secondary dominant — it's just the diatonic vi chord. The chromatic notes (C# in A7) are essential.
    • Overusing secondary dominants: Too many secondary dominants in a row can make the key feel ambiguous. Use them strategically for specific harmonic goals, not every single chord change.
    • Ignoring voice leading: The chromatic notes in secondary dominants (like C# in A7) must resolve properly. C# should move to D, not jump elsewhere. Poor voice leading destroys the effectiveness of the tonicization.
    • Using V/vii°: You cannot create a secondary dominant for the vii° chord because building a dominant 7th on its 5th would create a diminished 7th chord, not a dominant 7th. This doesn't function the same way.

    Why This Matters

    Secondary dominants are one of the most practical and widely used techniques in modern harmony because they:

    • Add harmonic interest without completely leaving the key
    • Create forward momentum — each secondary dominant pulls strongly to its target chord
    • Refresh familiar progressions — turn I-IV-V-I into I-V/IV-IV-V-I for instant sophistication
    • Enable smooth modulation — secondary dominants can be used to pivot to new keys
    • Bridge genres — they work in jazz, classical, blues, pop, rock, and electronic music

    Understanding secondary dominants unlocks thousands of classic songs and gives you a reliable tool for adding harmonic depth to your own music. Whether you're writing jazz, scoring a film, or producing pop, this is an essential technique.
