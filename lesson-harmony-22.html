    Theory

    Tritone Substitution: Jazz Reharmonization

    What You're Actually Making

    You are learning tritone substitution — a sophisticated reharmonization technique where you replace any dominant 7th chord with another dominant 7th chord located a tritone (6 half-steps) away. This creates smoother chromatic bass motion and a jazzier, more sophisticated sound.

    The magic of tritone substitution is that the two chords share the same 3rd and 7th (just inverted), so they function almost identically while providing completely different bass movement.

    Classic Example in C Major:

    Traditional:  Dm7 → G7  → Cmaj7
              ii    V     I

Tritone Sub:  Dm7 → Db7 → Cmaj7
              ii    bII7  I

    Instead of the bass moving G → C (up a 4th), the bass moves Db → C (down a half-step). This chromatic descent is smoother, jazzier, and more elegant.

    The Formula: How Tritone Substitution Works

    To create a tritone substitution:

    1. Identify the dominant 7th chord you want to replace (e.g., G7)
    2. Count up or down 6 half-steps (a tritone) from its root
    3. Build a dominant 7th chord on that new root
    4. Use it in place of the original dominant

    Example: Substituting G7 in C Major

    Original chord: G7 (G-B-D-F)
Tritone away from G: Db (6 half-steps: G → G# → A → Bb → B → C → Db)
Substitute chord: Db7 (Db-F-Ab-Cb/B)

G7  → Cmaj7  becomes  Db7 → Cmaj7

    The Shared Tones Secret:

    G7 chord:  G  - B  - D  - F
           │    │         │
           │    └─────────┼─── These are the same notes!
           │              │
Db7 chord: Db - F  - Ab - Cb (B enharmonic)

The 3rd of G7 (B) = the 7th of Db7 (Cb/B)
The 7th of G7 (F) = the 3rd of Db7 (F)

    Because the 3rd and 7th are the most important tones in a dominant 7th chord (they create the tritone tension that resolves to the tonic), swapping them doesn't change the fundamental harmonic function. Only the root and 5th are different.

    Chromatic Bass Motion: The Signature Sound

    The primary reason to use tritone substitution is to create chromatic bass lines. Instead of bass leaping by fourths or fifths, you get smooth half-step motion.

    Compare These Progressions:

    Without Tritone Substitution (Traditional ii-V-I):

    Dm7 → G7  → Cmaj7
D  →  G  →  C       (bass: D → G is up a 4th, G → C is up a 4th)

    With Tritone Substitution:

    Dm7 → Db7 → Cmaj7
D  →  Db →  C       (bass: D → Db → C is chromatic descent!)

    The bass line becomes a smooth chromatic walkdown: D → Db → C. This is the quintessential jazz sound — elegant, sophisticated, and effortlessly smooth.

    Extended Circle of Fifths with Tritone Subs:

    Traditional:  Em7 → A7  → Dm7 → G7  → Cmaj7
              iii   V/ii  ii    V     I

Tritone Sub:  Em7 → Eb7 → Dm7 → Db7 → Cmaj7
              iii   bII7  ii    bII7  I

Bass line: E → Eb → D → Db → C (continuous chromatic descent!)

    This creates one of the smoothest bass lines possible in Western harmony.

    Voice Leading in Tritone Substitution

    The voice leading in tritone substitution is remarkably smooth because of the shared 3rd and 7th.

    Example: Db7 → Cmaj7

    Db7 chord:     Db - F  - Ab - B
               ↓   ↓    ↓    ↓
Cmaj7 chord:   C  - E  - G  - B

Voice leading:
- Db (root) → C (root) [down half-step]
- F (3rd) → E (3rd) [down half-step]
- Ab (5th) → G (5th) [down half-step]
- B (7th) stays on B (7th of Cmaj7)

    Almost every voice moves by half-step or stays put — this is extremely smooth and easy to voice. Compare this to G7 → Cmaj7, where some voices jump by larger intervals.

    Why It Resolves: Even though Db7 is a tritone away from G7, the F and B (the shared 3rd/7th) still create tritone tension that wants to resolve inward to E and C (the root and 3rd of Cmaj7). The ear hears this as a dominant resolution, just with a different bass note.

    Substituting Secondary Dominants

    You can use tritone substitution on any dominant 7th chord, including all the secondary dominants you learned in Lesson 21!

    V/ii Tritone Sub:

    Traditional:  A7  → Dm7   (V/ii → ii)
Tritone Sub:  Eb7 → Dm7   (bII7 → ii)

Bass: Eb → D (chromatic descent)

    V/IV Tritone Sub:

    Traditional:  C7  → Fmaj7  (V/IV → IV in C major)
Tritone Sub:  Gb7 → Fmaj7  (bII7 → IV)

Bass: Gb → F (chromatic descent)

    V/V Tritone Sub (Double Tritone Sub):

    Traditional:  D7  → G7  → Cmaj7
              V/V   V     I

Tritone Sub:  Ab7 → Db7 → Cmaj7
              (V/V sub) (V sub) I

Bass: Ab → Db → C (chromatic descent in major 3rds, then half-step)

    This technique allows you to create chromatic bass motion in any progression that uses dominant 7th chords.

    Genre Applications

    Jazz Standards:

    • Tritone substitution is fundamental to bebop and modern jazz
    • Used extensively in ii-V-I progressions to create chromatic bass lines
    • Examples: "Satin Doll," "Autumn Leaves" (reharmonized versions), "Giant Steps"
    • Often combined with extended chords (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) for maximum sophistication

    Brazilian/Bossa Nova:

    • Bossa nova relies heavily on chromatic bass motion
    • Tritone subs create the smooth, lyrical bass lines characteristic of the genre
    • Example: "The Girl from Ipanema" uses tritone subs in the bridge

    R&B and Neo-Soul:

    • Modern R&B (D'Angelo, Erykah Badu, Robert Glasper) uses tritone subs for jazzy harmony
    • Creates smooth chord changes that feel more sophisticated than basic pop progressions
    • Often used in pre-choruses and bridges for harmonic lift

    Film Scoring:

    • Film composers use tritone subs to create tension or unexpected harmonic shifts
    • The chromatic bass descent can underscore mystery, elegance, or sophistication
    • Example: John Williams occasionally uses tritone subs in romantic themes

    Pop (Advanced):

    • Steely Dan, The Beatles (later albums), and Radiohead use tritone subs
    • Creates harmonic interest in choruses or bridges
    • Example: Steely Dan's "Aja" features multiple tritone substitutions

    Common Mistakes to Avoid

    • Using non-dominant 7th chords: Tritone substitution only works with dominant 7th chords (major triad + minor 7th). You cannot tritone sub a major 7th, minor 7th, or half-diminished 7th chord.
    • Forgetting the 7th: The substitute chord must be a dominant 7th, not just a major triad. Db major triad doesn't substitute for G7 — you need Db7.
    • Poor voice leading: Even though tritone subs have smooth voice leading potential, you must actually use smooth voice leading. Don't jump all the voices around — let them move by half-steps or common tones.
    • Overusing it: Tritone substitution creates a specific jazzy, chromatic sound. If you tritone sub every dominant chord, the progression loses its harmonic clarity. Use it strategically.
    • Not considering the melody: The substitute chord might clash with the melody. Check that the melody notes are chord tones or appropriate extensions (9th, 11th, 13th) of the Db7, not avoid notes.

    Advanced Technique: Tritone Substitution with Extensions

    Jazz musicians rarely play a plain Db7. They add extensions and alterations to make the chord even richer:

    Common Extensions for Tritone Subs:

    Db7 → Db7(b9)    - adds tension (Dbb/C, the b9)
Db7 → Db7(#11)   - adds color (G natural, the #11)
Db7 → Db7(b9,#11) - both tensions together (very jazzy)
Db7 → Db13       - adds 13th (Bb) for lush sound

    These extensions don't change the fundamental substitution — Db7(b9) still functions as a tritone sub for G7. They just add more color and complexity.

    Pro Tip: When voicing a tritone sub, prioritize the 3rd and 7th (F and B in Db7) since those are the shared tones that make the substitution work. Extensions are secondary.

    Why This Matters

    Tritone substitution is one of the most powerful reharmonization tools in modern music because it:

    • Creates chromatic bass motion — the signature sound of jazz and sophisticated harmony
    • Maintains harmonic function — the substitute chord resolves just like the original
    • Adds harmonic interest — transforms predictable progressions into sophisticated ones
    • Enables modulation — tritone subs can pivot to distant keys more smoothly
    • Works across genres — jazz, bossa nova, R&B, pop, film scoring all use this technique

    Whether you're reharmonizing a jazz standard, producing neo-soul, or scoring a film, tritone substitution gives you elegant, chromatic chord movement that sounds professional and polished. Combined with secondary dominants (Lesson 21), you now have the tools to reharmonize almost any progression.
