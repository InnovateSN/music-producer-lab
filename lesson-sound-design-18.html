What You'll Learn

Physical Modeling & Analog Emulation

Physical modeling uses mathematical models to simulate how real instruments produce sound—strings vibrating, air columns resonating, mallets striking. Unlike sampling, physical models respond dynamically to input: play harder, get brighter tone; change parameters, get different materials. Analog emulation applies similar principles to recreate vintage synthesizer circuits. Both approaches create expressive, realistic sounds that respond like real instruments.

Waveguide Synthesis

Waveguides model vibrating strings and tubes mathematically. Excitation (pluck, bow, breath) travels through the model, creating realistic acoustic behavior. Used in Pianoteq, AAS instruments, and Ableton Tension.

Resonator Models

Modal synthesis models resonant bodies—bells, bars, plates. Each mode (frequency) has its own decay. Adjust modal frequencies and decays to morph between materials: wood, metal, glass, membrane.

Analog Circuit Modeling

Component-level modeling recreates analog circuits: transistors, capacitors, transformers. This captures nonlinear behavior, subtle distortion, and "analog warmth" that simple digital doesn't match.

Expressive Control

Physical models respond to velocity, aftertouch, MPE. Map expression to model parameters: bow pressure, breath intensity, strike position. This creates performances that feel alive.

Why This Matters

  • Realistic Expression: Physical models respond naturally—more expressive than static samples.
  • Impossible Instruments: Create instruments that can't exist physically—10-foot guitars, glass pianos.
  • Vintage Authenticity: Analog emulations capture the character of classic gear affordably.
