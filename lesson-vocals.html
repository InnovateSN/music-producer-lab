Overview

The standard vocal chain.

Most professional vocal chains follow a similar order. Each step prepares the vocal for the next, building toward a polished, present sound.

1
Cleanup EQ (Subtractive)
Remove rumble, muddiness, and problem frequencies
2
Compression
Even out dynamics, add consistency and punch
3
De-esser
Tame harsh "S" and "T" sounds
4
Tone EQ (Additive)
Add presence, air, and character
5
Reverb / Delay (Send)
Add space and dimension

Step 3

De-essing.

Sibilance ("S", "T", "SH" sounds) can become harsh after compression. A de-esser is a frequency-specific compressor that tames these frequencies.

Step 4

Tone EQ (Additive).

Now that the vocal is clean and controlled, add the character. These are gentle boosts to enhance the natural qualities of the voice.

  • 100-200 Hz: Add warmth and body (use sparingly)
  • 2-4 kHz: Presence and intelligibility
  • 8-12 kHz: Air and sparkle

Less is more: 1-3 dB of boost is usually enough. Excessive boosting sounds unnatural and creates harshness.

Step 5

Reverb & Delay.

Space effects should be on a send/return‚Äîthis keeps the dry vocal intact while adding dimension.

  • Short reverb (0.8-1.5s): Adds glue without pushing the vocal back
  • Plate reverb: Classic vocal choice‚Äîsmooth and present
  • Slapback delay (80-120ms): Adds width and thickness
  • Long delay (quarter/eighth note): Creates space and interest

Pro tip: EQ your reverb return‚Äîcut low and high frequencies to prevent muddiness and harshness.

Your task

Process a vocal.

Take any vocal recording (or download a free acapella) and apply the chain:

  • Add a high-pass filter at 80-100 Hz
  • Find and cut any muddy frequencies (200-400 Hz)
  • Add compression: 4:1 ratio, medium attack, auto release
  • Add a de-esser targeting 5-7 kHz
  • Boost presence at 3 kHz and air at 10 kHz (subtle!)
  • Send to a reverb with 1.2s decay
