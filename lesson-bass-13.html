    Blues Bass: Turnarounds & Patterns

    What You're Actually Making

    You're programming a blues bass line — the foundational groove that built rock and roll, R&B, and blues. The 12-bar blues is one of the most important structures in Western music, and the bass line is its backbone. Unlike jazz walking (melodic and sophisticated) or funk (syncopated and percussive), blues bass is about solid, driving rhythm using root-fifth patterns.

    Blues bass masters like Willie Dixon (Chess Records), Duck Dunn (Stax/Booker T. & the M.G.'s), and Bill Black (Elvis Presley) created deceptively simple lines that anchor the entire band. The pattern you're building uses the classic root-fifth movement with a chromatic turnaround — that pull back to the beginning that makes you want to hear it loop forever.

    This lesson teaches you the DNA of rock, blues, country, rockabilly, and R&B bass. Master this, and you've mastered the foundation.

    The 12-Bar Blues Structure

    The 12-bar blues is a repeating 12-measure form built on three chords: I (tonic), IV (subdominant), and V (dominant). In the key of E:

    Standard 12-Bar Blues in E:

Bars 1-4:  E7  | E7  | E7  | E7  |   (I chord - 4 bars)
Bars 5-6:  A7  | A7  |              (IV chord - 2 bars)
Bars 7-8:  E7  | E7  |              (Back to I - 2 bars)
Bar 9:     B7  |                    (V chord - 1 bar)
Bar 10:    A7  |                    (IV chord - 1 bar)
Bar 11:    E7  |                    (I chord - 1 bar)
Bar 12:    B7  ||                   (V chord - Turnaround)

The form then repeats...

    Why This Works:

    • I chord (E7) — Home base, stable, relaxed
    • IV chord (A7) — Movement away, creates interest
    • V chord (B7) — Tension, pulls back to I
    • Turnaround (bar 12) — V chord creates maximum tension, resolving to I in bar 1

    This tension-and-release cycle is addictive. The turnaround makes you NEED to hear the I chord again, which is why blues loops feel so satisfying.

    Variations:

    • Quick-Change Blues: Bar 2 goes to IV (A7) instead of staying on I
    • Jazz Blues: Add ii-V progressions and substitutions
    • Minor Blues: Use minor I chord (Em instead of E7)

    Root-Fifth Movement: The Foundation

    Blues bass is built on the root-fifth pattern — alternating between the root and fifth of each chord:

    E7 chord:
Root: E  (MIDI 40 - E2)
Fifth: B (MIDI 47 - B2)

Pattern:
Beat 1: E (root)
Beat 3: B (fifth)
Beat 1: E (root)
Beat 3: B (fifth)

    Why Root-Fifth?

    • Harmonic clarity: Root establishes the chord, fifth reinforces it
    • Solid foundation: Gives the guitar/piano room to add color
    • Easy to lock with drums: Bass on 1 & 3, kick on 1 & 3, snare on 2 & 4
    • Leaves space: Only 2 notes per bar, not cluttered or busy

    When to Add More:

    Minimal (slow blues):  Root on 1, fifth on 3
Standard (medium):     Root-fifth alternating
Busy (fast/rockabilly): Root-fifth-sixth-fifth walking

    Start minimal. The greatest blues bassists play the fewest notes necessary. Less is more.

    Chromatic Approaches & Turnarounds

    The turnaround (bar 12) creates tension that pulls you back to bar 1. Blues bass uses chromatic walks to connect chords smoothly:

    1. Half-Step Approach to Chord Root:

    Going from E7 to A7:
... E - G♯ - A
       ↑
   Chromatic approach (G♯ → A)

Going back to E7:
... A - D♯ - E
       ↑
   Chromatic approach (D♯ → E)

    2. Classic Blues Turnaround (Bar 12):

    Bar 12: B7 (V chord)
Notes:  E - D♯ - E

The D♯ (one half-step below E) creates anticipation
When E hits on bar 1, it feels like home

Alternative turnarounds:
- E - F - F♯ - G (ascending chromatic)
- E - D - C♯ - C (descending to V)
- E - B - C - C♯ - D (walking up to IV)

    3. Walking Blues (Bars 1-4):

    Bar 1: E - G - A - B
Bar 2: E - G - A - B
Bar 3: E - G - A - B
Bar 4: E - G♯ (approach to A)

Walking through E blues scale creates melodic interest

    Chromatic approaches make blues bass sound intentional and sophisticated, not just repetitive root-fifth.

    Shuffle Feel: The Swing Rhythm

    Blues is traditionally played with a shuffle or swing feel — eighth notes are played "long-short-long-short" instead of evenly:

    Straight Eighths (rock, pop):
♪ ♪ ♪ ♪ = Even spacing

Shuffled Eighths (blues, jazz):
♪  ♪  ♪  ♪ = Long-short-long-short
(Triplet feel: ♪♪ ♪ ♪♪ ♪)

    Programming Shuffle:

    • Triplet Grid: Program on 1/12 or 1/24 grid (triplet subdivisions)
    • Swing Quantize: Apply 60-66% swing in your DAW
    • Manual Timing: Delay every 2nd 16th note by 10-15ms

    Tempo Considerations:

    Slow Blues: 60-80 BPM (heavy shuffle, lots of space)
Medium Blues: 90-110 BPM (standard blues feel)
Fast Blues: 120-140 BPM (rockabilly, shuffle groove)
Straight Blues: Any tempo (no shuffle, rock feel)

    Our 90 BPM pattern has a subtle shuffle. Practice locking the shuffle with the hi-hat for that classic blues pocket.

    Blues Bass Across Genres

    Delta Blues (1920s-40s):

    • Acoustic upright bass — Simple root-fifth, walking
    • Sparse, space-heavy — Follows vocal phrasing
    • Reference: Robert Johnson (implied bass in guitar)

    Chicago Blues (1950s-60s):

    • Willie Dixon — Upright and electric, solid root-fifth
    • Chess Records sound — Muddy Waters, Howlin' Wolf
    • Driving, amplified bass — Foundation for entire band

    Rock & Roll (1950s-60s):

    • Bill Black (Elvis) — Slap bass, percussive attack
    • Walking bass lines — Rockabilly swing feel
    • Energy and drive — Propels the groove forward

    Stax/Motown (1960s-70s):

    • Duck Dunn (Booker T) — Pocket-oriented, minimal
    • James Jamerson (Motown) — Melodic blues with soul
    • Greasy, behind-the-beat — Laid-back R&B feel

    Blues Rock (1960s-70s):

    • John Paul Jones (Led Zeppelin) — Heavy, distorted blues
    • Jack Bruce (Cream) — Jazz-influenced blues bass
    • Geezer Butler (Black Sabbath) — Blues bass meets heavy metal

    Modern Applications:

    • Hip-Hop — 12-bar blues sampled (Dr. Dre, OutKast)
    • Indie/Alternative — The Black Keys, Jack White use blues bass
    • Electronic — Blues scales in house and techno (Moodymann)

    Blues Bass Techniques

    1. Ghost Notes (Motown/Stax):

    Add muted percussive notes between main notes for groove:

    E (100) - ghost (50) - B (90) - ghost (50)

Adds texture without cluttering the low end

    2. Slap Technique (Rockabilly):

    Percussive thumb slap on upright bass:

    Slap on beats 2 & 4 (where snare hits)
Creates percussive attack, adds energy
Famous in: Elvis, Johnny Cash, Carl Perkins

    3. String Bends (Blues Rock):

    Bend up to target notes for expressive phrasing:

    D (bend up to) E
Adds vocal-like expression, bluesy character

    4. Chromatic Walks:

    Connect chord changes with half-step motion:

    E - E♭ - D - C♯ - C
Creates melodic movement, sophisticated blues

    5. Dynamic Expression:

    • Verse: Play soft (velocity 60-80), support vocal
    • Chorus: Play strong (velocity 90-110), drive groove
    • Solo: Walk or add melodic fills, interact with soloist
    • Turnaround: Build tension, crescendo into bar 1

    Common Mistakes

    • Too Busy: Playing too many notes kills the blues feel. Blues bass is about space and groove, not technical display. If you're playing more than 4-6 notes per bar, simplify.
    • Wrong Turnaround: Not using the V chord (B7) in bar 12 makes the loop feel incomplete. The turnaround creates the tension that pulls you back. Without it, the form drags.
    • No Shuffle: Playing straight eighths when the song needs shuffle makes it sound stiff and robotic. Listen to the drums — if they're shuffling, you shuffle.
    • Ignoring Dynamics: Playing every note at the same velocity sounds lifeless. Blues is about emotion — follow the vocalist, build during solos, drop back in verses.
    • Overplaying During Vocals: Blues bass supports the vocal. When the singer is telling the story, play minimal. Save your fills for instrumental breaks.

    Why This Matters

    The 12-bar blues is the foundation of modern popular music. Rock, R&B, soul, funk, hip-hop, and country all evolved from blues. Learning blues bass gives you:

    • Structural understanding: The I-IV-V progression appears in 80% of popular songs
    • Groove mastery: Blues shuffle and pocket are the basis for all groove music
    • Harmonic ear: Understanding tension and release through chord movement
    • Genre fluency: Blues vocabulary translates to rock, R&B, country, and jazz
    • Historical context: Understanding the music that built modern production

    Even electronic producers sample blues. Tracks like Dr. Dre's "Still D.R.E." use blues-based bass lines. The Black Keys and Jack White built careers on modern blues production. Understanding blues makes you a more versatile and culturally aware producer.

    Blues teaches you to say more with less — the mark of a professional bassist.
